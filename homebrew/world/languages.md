# Languages of the Sealed Age

What's actually spoken, read, and remembered in the world Fallowton sits in. This page **overlays** the 2024 RAW languages list in [[languages]] (wiki/concepts) — keep that as the baseline; the additions and tweaks here are campaign-specific.

> See also: [[weave-strain-magic]] (Dawnweave / Sealing), [[world/calendar]] (month names carry buried Dawnweave roots), [[languages]] (RAW baseline).

The shape: most people speak one tongue and curse in another. Scholars read three. Sister Elra chants in something older than the kingdom and won't say where she learned it.

## Living tongues (spoken today; PCs may know by default)

These are what villagers, merchants, and crown officials actually use. RAW baseline applies — every PC starts with **Crown Common** plus at least 2 additional standard languages.

| Language | Who speaks it | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Crown Common** | Everyone in the kingdom, more or less | The lingua franca. What Fallowton calls *"plain talk."* Crown-standardized spelling and tax forms; spoken dialect varies wildly by province. |
| **Hearthtongue** | Fallowton and the surrounding provinces — the "old crown country" | Folk-tongue of the inland farms. Older than Crown Common; the crown tolerates it. Songs, oaths, lullabies, the names of fields. Goodwife Bessa swears in it. |
| **Reach-Cant** | The forest country past the west, the hill villages | Clipped, consonant-heavy. Outsiders find it rude; speakers find that funny. |
| **Saltspeech** | Coastal towns, river-traders, anyone who moves goods by water | A trade pidgin — half Crown Common, half borrowed nouns, all numbers. If a merchant haggles with you in Saltspeech, you've been marked as a sucker. |
| **Wayfarer's Cant** | Carters, drovers, traveling fairs, smugglers | Not Thieves' Cant — broader, less criminal. Hand-signs, road-marks on fence posts, a vocabulary for weather and bad roads. |
| **Goblin, Orc, Giant, Draconic, Dwarvish, Elvish, Gnomish, Halfling, Common Sign Language** | RAW — affirmed | As in [[languages]]. Goblin and Orc are the monstrous tongues a Fallowton-area PC is most likely to encounter in play. |

The rare languages (Abyssal, Celestial, Deep Speech, Infernal, Primordial, Sylvan, Undercommon) work as RAW. Druidic and Thieves' Cant remain class-locked.

## Liturgical and scholarly tongues (read, not lived in)

These are written languages. Spoken only in ritual, study, or contempt of court.

### Old Common — Sister Elra's chant language

The direct ancestor of Crown Common, last spoken daily about four hundred years before the Sealing. Survives now only in **priest-chants, blessing-formulas, and the bound prayer-books the village shrine keeps locked.** Sister Elra leads the Lampset Vigil prayer in Old Common; she leads the Crowning blessing in it; she swears under her breath in it when she thinks no one is listening.

To a Crown Common speaker, Old Common **sounds like rhythmic prose** — the cadence is familiar, root-words flicker recognizable, but the grammar is wrong and half the vocabulary is gone. You can tell it's a *prayer*. You can't tell what it's *for*.

> **[HOUSE] Old Common at the table.** A PC who knows Old Common (see *Proficiency*, below) reading or hearing it makes a **Religion check DC 12**: on success, full translation; on failure, the gist plus one phrase verbatim. A PC who *doesn't* know Old Common gets only the rhythm. **Comprehend Languages works normally** — it's an extant tongue, just an old one.

### Office-Hand

The crown's bureaucratic script. Compressed, abbreviated, deliberately ugly. Tax assessments, sealed warrants, the [[crown-and-office]] roster of permitted practitioners. Designed to be **slow to forge and slower to read aloud.** A village magistrate reads Office-Hand. A literate farmer doesn't bother.

### Liturgical Calendar Script

The script the Sealing-Day prayer cycle is written in — also the source of the month-name etymologies recorded in [[world/calendar]]. Same alphabet as Old Common but heavily ligatured; only priests and a few scholars can parse it at speed. PCs with proficiency in this can read calendar-ritual texts and the inscriptions on shrine bells.

## Lost / mythic tongues (used in story; not understood)

These are languages no one alive speaks. A PC may **recognize** them on sight; only specific backgrounds and feats can read them; **none can be spoken safely.**

### Dawnweave Architect Script (the Converter glyphs)

Carved on the walls of the House of First Light. Stamped into the seals. Inlaid in the floor of every Converter chamber. **The language the people who built the machines used to instruct the machines.**

What it looks like: angular, deeply incised, arranged in concentric rings rather than lines. Glyphs cluster around function — power, binding, channeling, naming. Some are clearly verbs. Some seem to be the *names of victims*, repeated in the margins like a tally.

> **[HOUSE] What a trained PC can do with it.** A PC with the **Ruin Scholar background** (see [[ruin-scholar]]) or who's taken time in-fiction to study Dawnweave inscriptions can **read Architect Script silently** — extracting names, purposes, warnings, command sequences. They **cannot pronounce it aloud safely.** Reading is study; speaking is *activation.* Pronouncing a glyph cluster in the House of First Light is functionally casting a 1st-level spell with the strain rules from [[weave-strain-magic]] — including the Overreach roll if at strain 2+. **Comprehend Languages will translate written Architect Script as if it were plain text — but does not let the caster speak it.** A spell that translates speech-into-speech (e.g. *Tongues*) **fails outright** on Architect Script: the words have no meaning the listener's mind can hold without paying for it.

### The Hollowing Tongue (mythic)

The language the Nameless Hunger — the **Hollow Star** — is said to *almost* speak. Appears in three places: the deepest carvings under the Converter (single glyphs only, scratched in over the original Architect Script as if by something inside the wall); two children's songs in Hearthtongue whose nonsense-syllables aren't quite nonsense; and the noise Mordren makes when he wakes from the bad dreams.

> **[HOUSE]** Comprehend Languages, Tongues, Legend Lore, and divinations **fail** on the Hollowing Tongue. A caster who tries takes **+1 Weave Strain** for the attempt and the DM rolls a hidden Awakening Clock tick. The Hollowing Tongue is not *unknown* — it is *unmeaning.* There is nothing in the words to translate.

### Godspeech (mythic)

The language the gods used to seal the world-magic core. Recorded nowhere; remembered in no human mind. Appears in legend as *"the word that closed the door."* A few priestly orders claim fragments. None of the claims agree. **Cannot be learned, written, or cast in.** Useful to the DM as the answer to *"why doesn't anyone just open the seal?"* — because no one alive knows the language the lock was made in.

## How language proficiency works at the table

RAW baseline (see [[languages]]): Common + 2 standard, plus class/species/background grants. That stands.

[HOUSE] additions:

- **Crown Common** = the RAW "Common." Pick this; it's automatic.
- **Hearthtongue, Reach-Cant, Saltspeech, Wayfarer's Cant** = each counts as a standard-tier choice. PCs from the region likely know Hearthtongue without spending a slot if the DM agrees at session zero.
- **Old Common** requires either the **Acolyte** or **Sage** background **plus** the **Linguist** feat, **or** DM-granted via story (a season studying under Sister Elra; a found tutor in the city). Never a free pick at character creation without a hook.
- **Office-Hand** requires Noble, Magistrate-adjacent, or Sage background — or a feat slot.
- **Liturgical Calendar Script** requires Acolyte and time in-fiction studying. Counts as one rare-language slot.
- **Dawnweave Architect Script** requires the **Ruin Scholar background** or DM-granted study time. **Read only — never spoken.**
- **Hollowing Tongue / Godspeech**: cannot be learned. PCs may recognize them on a Religion DC 15 / History DC 18 check.

**Reading vs speaking** matters in this campaign. Some tongues (Architect Script, the calendar script) are scholarly artifacts — you read them off a wall, you don't have a conversation in them. Pronouncing a research tongue aloud is what *gets people killed in stories*. Take the warning seriously when the DM repeats it.

## DM use — where languages matter at the table

The point isn't to gate content behind language proficiency. It's to make the world feel layered. Six moments to drop language into play:

1. **The shrine.** Sister Elra finishes the Crowning blessing in Old Common; the PC without it hears rhythmic prose, the PC with it hears the line *"may the seal hold."* That's all. Don't over-explain. Let the line ring.
2. **The Converter wall.** PCs enter the House of First Light; the Ruin Scholar reads a tally of names scratched into the Architect Script and goes quiet. Other PCs see the same wall and see geometry. The split is the point.
3. **The sealed warrant.** An Office of Sealed Practice rider hands the magistrate a document in Office-Hand. The PCs see the seal; they don't read the warrant unless one of them can. Tension lives in not-knowing.
4. **The Hollowing fragment.** A child in Fallowton sings a song with one verse no one's grandmother taught them. A Sinkhole Witness PC recognizes it as the noise from a dream. *No roll.* Just the chill.
5. **Lampset Vigil.** Lampset 12; Sister Elra lights the western candle and murmurs in Old Common. The PC who knows it catches *"the first light, the last light, the light that was paid for."* History DC 15 then unlocks why those words are wrong. (See [[world/calendar]] §Holidays.)
6. **The locked door.** A Dawnweave seal blocks the way. Reading the glyphs (Architect Script proficiency) tells the PCs the *command* — but not how to say it without bleeding. Now they decide whether to pay the price.

Treat languages like the calendar: a texture, not a quiz. Drop one moment per session. Don't announce that a language is being spoken — let the players notice.
