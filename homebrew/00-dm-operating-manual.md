# DM Operating Manual

> The manual that lives behind every module. Read it before your first session. Reread §1 the morning of every session forever.

This is the *cross-module* personal manual — when to roll vs. just narrate, how much spotlight per player, how to improvise rulings, when to break your own clocks. Module-specific play scripts (e.g. [`docs/dm-handbook/module-1-*`](../docs/dm-handbook/)) tell you **what** happens in a given session. This file tells you **how** to run any session.

**Scope of this file.** Opinionated defaults written for this campaign (Fallowton). Where I disagree with BR-2024's defaults or feel they are weak, I bake in tweaks here and flag them as **[HOUSE]**. You can reject any [HOUSE] callout and keep the section above it.

**File status.** Sections 1–2 below are v1-committed. Sections 3–10 (listed at end) are drafted incrementally, one at a time, in collaboration. See `sections-complete` in the frontmatter.

## 1 · The Job

You are not the players' adversary. You are not the rulebook. You are not the author of their story.

You are the **world's voice** — you describe what *is*, you do not decide what they *do*. And you are the **table's editor** — you cut scenes that drag, keep the camera on the right person, and end the session when the moment lands.

Three reframings to keep close:

1. **The world is the obstacle — not you.** When the players want something hard, the answer comes from the fiction (Bran is afraid; the door is locked from outside; the goblin is faster), never from your authorial preference. You do not "want" them to fail or succeed. The world does what the world does.
2. **The dice are the judge.** When the stakes are uncertain *and* interesting, you hand the verdict to the dice. You do not pre-decide outcomes. You also do not roll dice for things whose outcome doesn't matter.
3. **The table is the audience and the cast.** Every player gets time on stage. Every player goes home having done something only their character could have done.

If you forget every other rule in this manual, those three keep you safe.

### The morning-of mantra (read aloud before any session)

> *I am the world's voice. I am the table's editor. I am not the adversary, not the rulebook, not the author. The world is the obstacle. The dice are the judge. Every player gets a moment that only their character could have.*

Fifteen seconds. Centers you before the players arrive.

## 2 · The Interference Dial

The single biggest question new DMs ask themselves at the table is *"am I interfering too much, or not enough?"* This section answers it.

The answer is: **interference has four discrete levels.** Your job is to know which level you're at, and to step up or down on purpose, not by accident.

### The four levels

| Level | Name | What it is | How often |
|---|---|---|---|
| **0** | NARRATE | You describe what the world does. Players act. Dice judge. You stay out. | ~60% of session |
| **1** | ADJUDICATE | A player tries something; you decide whether it's automatic, impossible, or needs a roll. | ~30% of session |
| **2** | INTERVENE | You insert something the players didn't ask for: NPC action, clock tick, complication. | ~10% of session |
| **3** | OVERRIDE | You break the contract: undo a roll, retcon a scene, force a PC's hand. | **Almost never.** Twice per campaign at most. |

The percentages are aspirational, not strict. The point is the **shape**: you spend most of your time describing, some of your time judging, a small slice intervening, and almost none overriding.

### Level 0 — NARRATE

You describe the world. The players act on what you described. You shut up and listen.

- **Use when:** default state. Beginning of scenes. After a player acts. When the world is doing what the world does.
- **Phrases that work:**
  - *"The door is locked. What do you do?"*
  - *"The orc swings — that hits. Roll for damage."*
  - *"Sister Elra looks at you. She says nothing."*
  - *"You enter the room. There is a smell of wet iron and old smoke. There is one window, shuttered."*
- **The trap:** *monologuing.* If you've been narrating for 90+ seconds without any player input, you've slipped into authorship. **CUT.** Even mid-sentence is fine. "—and the door creaks. What do you do?"
- **The other trap:** *passivity.* If the players are silent for 30+ seconds after you finished narrating, they probably didn't catch a hook. Drop a verb: *"There's a sealed letter on the desk."* (now they have a target).

### Level 1 — ADJUDICATE

A player says "I want to X." You decide: automatic? impossible? dice?

**The three-question test (run this in your head in <5 seconds):**

1. **Is the outcome genuinely uncertain?** If yes → continue. If no, just narrate the outcome.
2. **Are the stakes interesting?** If yes → continue. If no (failure means "you try again with no cost"), just say it works.
3. **Does a failure produce an interesting story, not a dead end?** If yes → roll. If no → re-frame the check so a failure costs something other than information (time, a resource, a complication).

If all three are yes: name the skill, name the DC, ask for the roll.

- **Phrases that work:**
  - *"Roll Investigation, DC 12."* (decisive, no hesitation)
  - *"That works automatically."* (free win for good idea)
  - *"It can't be done from where you stand. You'd need to climb first."* (the world answers)
- **The trap:** *over-rolling.* New DMs ask for rolls when they're nervous. If you don't know what failure looks like, don't roll. Use [HOUSE] rule below.

> **[HOUSE] Default: don't roll.** Of the three test questions, the hardest is #3. If you can't picture an interesting failure in 3 seconds, the answer is: *no roll, just give them what they tried for, and move on.* Rolls are not a substitute for permission. Most checks in a session should come from the players asking *"can I roll for this?"* — not from you announcing one.

### Level 2 — INTERVENE

You insert something into the fiction the players didn't ask for. An NPC speaks unprompted. A clock fires. A complication walks through the door.

- **Use when:**
  - Pacing needs help (scene has been spinning for 10+ min on one beat).
  - A clock you set is overdue to tick (Day Clock, Awakening Clock, deadline).
  - The world needs to remind players it's alive (off-screen NPCs have lives too).
  - A player has had no spotlight in 20+ minutes — intervene to hand them a moment.
- **Phrases that work:**
  - *"While you're talking to Bessa, the door bangs open and Garret runs in — he's holding flour-streaked hands and saying your name."*
  - *"Mordren stands up. 'We are running out of light.'"*
  - *"The Sunset Bell rings, twice, across the village. Everyone in the room turns toward the door."*
- **The trap:** *railroading hides here.* Intervention is the power tool that breaks campaigns when overused. Two rules:
  1. **Every intervention must fire from the fiction**, not from your authorial impatience. If you intervene to push the plot forward because *you* are bored, the players will feel the hand on their shoulder.
  2. **Maximum twice per session.** If you find yourself wanting a third, ring an existing clock instead — that's what they're for.

### Level 3 — OVERRIDE

You break the table's contract with the dice and the fiction. You undo a roll, declare a death didn't happen, retcon a scene, force a PC's hand.

- **Use when:** almost never. Reserved for:
  - **Table safety.** A player triggered something that needs to back out (real-world distress, accidentally-traumatic content). Override silently and check in after.
  - **System-breaking dice.** A single unlucky roll has produced a TPK on what was supposed to be a setup encounter (see Cardinal Rule 2 below).
  - **New-player rescue.** Session 1, level 1, a brand-new player is about to die before they've played their character. Override once, transparently, never again with that player.
- **Phrases that work:**
  - *"Hold on — let me take that back. That roll didn't happen. We rewind 30 seconds."*
  - *"You're at 0 HP — but I'm pulling that punch. The blow grazes you. You're at 1."* (transparent, used once)
- **The trap:** if you find yourself wanting to override regularly, the world you designed is producing outcomes you don't want. Fix the design between sessions, not the dice during.

### The Yes Ladder (the player's-side translator)

When a player says *"I want to X"*, the five answers in order of preference are:

| Rung | Answer | When |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | **YES, AND** — "and the way you described it makes the moment land harder." | Player did something creative that fits the fiction. Free, joyful. Use as often as possible. |
| **2** | **YES, BUT** — "but it costs the next 10 minutes / a torch / a strain." | Player wants something reasonable; world charges a price. Free with friction. |
| **3** | **YES, IF** — "if you pass this check." | The outcome is uncertain and interesting. The dice decide. |
| **4** | **NO, BECAUSE** — "the door is bound with iron from the outside." | The world answers, not you. Always include the *why*. |
| **5** | **NO.** | Reserved for fictional impossibility, table safety, or destroying another player's agency. |

**Aim for the top rungs.** A good session has ~5 YES-ANDs, ~3 YES-BUTs, ~6 YES-IFs, ~2 NO-BECAUSEs, ~0 bare NOs. If you hit a bare NO twice in a session, debrief yourself afterward: what was I protecting?

### When to step UP the dial (intervene more)

- Real-world time is 30+ min over the scene's pacing budget → INTERVENE to cut. Ring the bell, send the messenger, make an NPC stand up.
- A player has been silent for 15+ min → INTERVENE to hand them a moment ("Bessa turns to you — *Garret says you used to fix the mill stones. Can you look at this?*").
- A clock you set is overdue → INTERVENE to fire it visibly.
- The conversation has gone in a circle for 3+ player-turns → INTERVENE to break the loop ("As you debate, the front door of the inn opens — ").

### When to step DOWN the dial (intervene less)

- A player just announced an intention you weren't planning for → NARRATE, don't override.
- A player rolled poorly on a check whose failure is interesting → NARRATE the failure, don't soften it.
- A player did something you didn't expect, with great roleplay, that bypasses a planned scene → say **YES-AND**, never explain why it was technically forbidden. Cut the planned scene. The bypass *is* the story now.
- You're about to say "actually, the rule is..." → swallow it. Rule it now, look it up between sessions.

### The two cardinal rules

> **[HOUSE]** These are the strict version of dice ethics for this campaign. Some tables allow more DM dice manipulation than this. Ours doesn't.

**Cardinal Rule 1 — Never fudge dice against the players. Ever.**
If they roll a natural 1, the 1 stands. If the monster crits, the crit stands. If a trap kills them outright, the trap kills them outright. The dice are sacred *because they are sacred to both sides.* The moment players suspect you're rolling against them in secret, the campaign is over even if they keep showing up.

**Roll your dice in the open** whenever possible. Hidden rolls are for Silent checks ([per `module-1-checks.md`](../docs/dm-handbook/module-1-checks.md)) and atmosphere only.

**Cardinal Rule 2 — You may fudge FOR the players in exactly two situations, and only with a transparent pull.**

1. A single unlucky roll has produced a TPK in an encounter that was *not designed for stakes that high* (e.g. a "setup" combat in Session 1 where one bad crit chain wipes the party).
2. A brand-new player's first character is dying in Session 1 before they've had a meaningful turn.

In both cases:

- Do it **once per campaign maximum.**
- **Say what you're doing.** "I'm pulling that punch. The blow grazes you. You're at 1 HP." Transparent. Visible. Earnest.
- **Don't make it a habit.** If you find yourself wanting to fudge a second time, the encounter design is wrong. Redesign, don't re-fudge.

## 3 · When to Roll

Building on §2's three-question test, this section is the full dice philosophy. Where §2 told you the *philosophy* of when to roll, §3 gives you the *mechanics*.

### The hierarchy of resolution

For any player action, in order of preference (try the lowest rung that works):

1. **Don't roll.** Auto-succeed, auto-fail, or doesn't need resolution.
2. **Roll once.** A single d20 against a DC; single outcome.
3. **Roll with consequence laddering.** Same roll; different outcomes by margin (beat by 5+, exact, miss by 5+).
4. **Group check.** Half the party needs to succeed.
5. **Contest.** Opposed rolls; ties favor the defender.

### The DC ladder

| DC | Difficulty | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Trivial | Almost never roll — just succeed. |
| 10 | Easy | Most low-stress checks; in-class skill. |
| 13 | Default | "This is interesting." Your most common DC. |
| 15 | Hard | A skilled PC's signature attempt. |
| 18 | Very hard | Specialist on a stretch. |
| 20 | Legendary | Don't gate Module 1 content here. |

**Probabilities at level 1** (PC with proficiency + +3 ability = 1d20+5):

| DC | Success rate |
|---|---|
| 10 | 80% |
| 13 | 65% |
| 15 | 55% |
| 18 | 40% |
| 20 | 30% |

> **[HOUSE] The fixed-rung rule.** When you set a DC, pick from `{10, 13, 15, 18}` unless there's a specific reason to deviate. Two reasons:
>
> 1. **Removes decision fatigue.** You don't have to choose between seven numbers under table pressure.
> 2. **Teaches the players the rungs.** After three sessions, "DC 15" carries weight — they know it's *very hard*. DC 11 vs DC 12 reads identically at the table; DC 13 vs DC 15 doesn't.
>
> DC 12 from BR-2024 is fine when narrating ("you'll need at least 12"), but internally, the DM uses 10/13/15/18.

### Passive scores

**Passive = 10 + ability modifier + proficiency (if applicable).** Most commonly Perception, Insight, Investigation.

Compare passive directly to DC — no roll needed.

**Use passive when:**

- The player isn't actively rolling. (Default for Perception walking through a corridor.)
- Silent checks ([per `module-1-checks.md`](../docs/dm-handbook/module-1-checks.md)) — atmosphere, dread, hidden info on a fail-narrate-nothing basis.
- "Does anyone notice X?" without alerting the players you asked.

**Don't use passive when:**

- The player has explicitly said they're examining/searching — they get an active Investigation roll.
- The check needs to feel decisive at the table. Let them roll for the drama.

### Group checks

Half the party (rounded up) needs to succeed for the group to succeed. The math: in a party of 4, two must pass. The clumsy character drags the average — that's the point.

**Use group checks for:**

- **Stealth** — the whole party sneaking past guards.
- **Travel hazards** — crossing a river, navigating ice.
- **Survival** — finding the path through wilderness.
- **Forced march** — Con checks to resist exhaustion.

**Don't use group checks for:**

- **Knowledge** — only one person needs to know. Best knower rolls.
- **Social** — one person leads. Others take the **Help** action.
- **Combat** — initiative + individual rolls.

### Contests

Two opposed rolls. Higher result wins; ties favor the defender.

Common contests:

- **Grapple:** attacker Athletics vs defender's choice of Athletics or Acrobatics.
- **Shove:** Athletics vs Athletics or Acrobatics.
- **Hide vs notice** (when the hider knows they're being searched for): active Stealth vs active Perception.
- **Sleight of Hand vs Perception** for theft attempts the target might notice.

**In combat:** both sides roll. **Outside combat:** sometimes the DM rolls for the NPC vs the PC's passive (silent check).

### Advantage / disadvantage

Roll 2d20, take higher (advantage) or lower (disadvantage).

- **Sources never stack.** Multiple sources of advantage are still just one advantage.
- **They cancel 1:1.** Any advantage + any disadvantage → plain d20 (no matter how many of each).

**Sources of advantage worth knowing:**

- **Help action** (one ally; takes their action to grant).
- **Hidden attacker's first attack** (you were unseen).
- **Heroic Inspiration** (BR-2024 free re-roll-as-advantage from Humans + some feats).
- **Specific spells, features, items** (e.g., [[guidance]], [[bless]]).
- **Targeting a Restrained, Paralyzed, Stunned, Prone-within-5ft creature** (see [[conditions-overview]]).

> **[HOUSE] Don't grant ad-hoc advantage for "good roleplay."** Use the **YES-AND** rung from §2 instead — give them the *thing*, not the *bonus*. Advantage is a mechanical tool with mechanical sources. Roleplay is rewarded narratively (lower DC, fuller info on success, a YES-AND result), not by stacking bonuses.

### [HOUSE] Roll-first, narrate-after (out of combat)

When a check is going to happen, ask for the roll *before* you describe the action in detail. This prevents the trap of painting a beautiful 20-second action only to roll a 3 and back-narrate.

> **Wrong:**
> *"You crouch behind the barrel, ease the door open with your shoulder, slip the knife into the lock — roll Stealth."*
>
> **Right:**
> *"Roll Stealth. ... 14. Yes — you slip past the threshold without a sound. The hallway opens, dust thick on the floorboards."*

The dice tell you what kind of moment to narrate. Trust them.

**Exception:** mid-combat actions and roleplay-heavy moments. There the choice *is* the drama; the roll is consequence. Narrate first.

### [HOUSE] Stakes-required for non-attack combat rolls

Inside combat, every non-attack roll must have a stake more interesting than *"do you fail and waste your turn."* When a player wants to do something cinematic — swing on a chandelier, knock over a brazier, push a cart at the enemy — the answer is **YES-IF**, but the **IF** specifies what failure *costs*:

- *"Yes if you pass Acrobatics DC 13. Fail = you fall prone in the brazier's path."*
- *"Yes if you pass Athletics DC 12. Fail = the cart pins you instead of them."*

This rewards creativity with consequences worth rolling for, and protects against players cycling through "I'll try Strength to push the thing" until something works.

### Creative skill + ability combinations

Players will ask to pair a skill with a non-default ability. The answer is almost always **YES** if it makes in-fiction sense.

| Player says | Allow | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "I intimidate them with raw strength." | Str (Intimidation) | Physical menace is intimidating. |
| "I lead the group climb with morale." | Cha (Athletics) | Inspiring + coordinating counts. |
| "I improvise a prayer to calm them." | Wis (Performance) | Performance with religious context. |
| "I read their character to predict their move." | Wis (Insight) | (Default; this is just Insight.) |

**The rule:** if skill + ability makes fiction sense, allow it. Player uses *their* proficiency (whether they're proficient in the *skill*); DM picks *the ability* that fits the moment. This rewards creativity at the dice level without breaking the system.

See [`wiki/quick_reference.md`](../wiki/) for the canonical list of skills + their default abilities. Use the BR-2024 example: "Strength (Intimidation) when intimidating through a show of physical strength."

### When NOT to roll — by category

| Situation | What to do instead |
|---|---|
| You've already decided to give them the info | Just tell them. No roll. |
| Trained PC + trivial task (DC ≤10 with proficiency) | Auto-succeed. |
| Failure = "try again, no cost" | No consequence, no roll. Skip. |
| Safe area, no clock running | Narrate. No checks unless asked. |
| You can't picture what failure looks like | Don't roll. ([Per §2 three-question test #3.](#2--the-interference-dial)) |
| Player is in-character, DM has no stake in the outcome | YES-AND. |

### When to call for a check the player didn't ask for

Rare, but legitimate. Use only when:

- The PC is approaching something a normal observer would notice — Perception, Insight, Investigation.
- The PC is taking damage or being affected by an environmental hazard — Con save, Dex save.
- The PC is being targeted by a hidden effect — saving throw.
- The PC is doing something mundane that becomes consequential because of a clock — their lockpicking is now timed.

**In all four:** roll **before** narrating the trigger. The result tells you what to say.

### [HOUSE] Heroic Inspiration economy expansion

BR-2024 grants Heroic Inspiration to Humans on a long rest, plus a few feats. We expand the earning conditions and tighten the spend.

**The DM awards Heroic Inspiration when a player:**

1. **Plays mechanically suboptimally but in-character.** They refuse healing because of an oath. They speak up at the wrong moment because their character would. They take the harder choice for the right reason.
2. **Sets up another player.** Ad-libs a feed line for someone else's catchphrase. Hands the spotlight back. Asks "what does your character think of this?" mid-scene.
3. **Arrives prepared.** Notes, in-fiction sketches, character art, music suggestions for the session.

**Limit:** 1 award per player per session. You don't have to give one to everyone every time.

**Spend:** a player can spend Heroic Inspiration to **re-roll any d20 before the result is read.** They take the **second** roll, not the better of the two.

This is a positive-reinforcement tool that doesn't break dice purity (Cardinal Rule 1, §2). It rewards what you want at the table without you having to fudge anything.

## 4 · Time & Pacing

The single biggest cause of new-DM sessions feeling "off" is **time slipping without the DM noticing.** A 10-minute scene takes 40. The cliffhanger lands in real-time 4-hour mark instead of 3-hour. Players check their phones during transitions. This section is the tools to fix that.

### The three time scales

You manage three clocks at once. They can drift apart deliberately. **Drift you don't notice** is the problem.

| Scale | What it measures | Who watches it |
|---|---|---|
| **Real time** | Wall clock at the table. ~3.5 hours total. | The DM. Players notice only when it overruns. |
| **In-game time** | What time it is in the world right now. | The DM tracks; players ask occasionally. |
| **Narrative arc** | Beats of the session (anchor → choices → spike → land). | Everyone unconsciously. The shape they feel. |

A 5-minute scene at the table can compress 3 in-game hours of travel. A 30-minute roleplay scene might cover 15 in-game minutes of conversation. **Mismatch isn't a bug; it's the medium.** Tracking all three keeps you from getting trapped inside one of them.

### The pacing budget

~3.5 hours of real time per session. Subtract:

- 10 min setup / "where were we" recap
- 5 min end-of-session debrief & logging

= **~3 hours of actual play.**

Divide by your acts (3–4 per session) → ~45–60 min per act.

Every module gets a pacing budget table like the one in [`module-1-dm-flow.md`](../docs/dm-handbook/module-1-dm-flow.md) ("Pacing budget"). Build it before the session, not during.

> **[HOUSE] The 60% rule.** Plan content for **60% of your time.** The other 40% is reserved for: player decisions taking longer than expected, social scenes that catch fire, dice doing weird things, and the inevitable side-quest invented at the table.
>
> If you plan for 100%, you will always overrun.
> If you plan for 60% and players don't fill the rest, you have buffer for the cliffhanger to breathe.

### The speed lever

Every scene has a default pace. You can **compress or expand it in real time without players noticing**, using these tools.

**To EXPAND a scene** (you want it to take longer at the table):

- **Ask more questions.** *"What do you do? Specifically?"* *"How are you holding the dagger when you do that?"* *"Are you looking at her, or past her?"*
- **Bring more NPCs in.** Each named NPC reaction adds 30+ seconds. Have Bessa lean in to listen; have Henric quietly stand up.
- **Layer the senses.** Smell of damp wool, distant hammering, the way the firelight catches one corner. Sensory description slows the camera.
- **Have an NPC ask the PCs about themselves.** *"You said you were from where, again?"* Players love being asked.

**To COMPRESS a scene** (you want to move faster):

- **Narrate transitions.** *"You finish your drinks, pay, and step out into the dusk. The wind has changed."* Skip the goodbye.
- **Skip rolls that don't matter.** *"You find what you were looking for."* No Investigation roll. Move.
- **Use montage language.** *"Over the next hour, you check each shed in turn. The third one has —"*
- **Cut to the moment that matters.** *"Three hours later — you're at the wizard's door."*

The lever lets you keep the **narrative arc** on time even when real time is slipping.

### Scene-cutting like film editing

Most new DMs let scenes end when the players run out of things to say. **That's too late.** Cut the scene a beat *before* they'd want to leave.

A scene should end on one of four shapes:

1. **A decision.** *"So we descend? Yes? Then it's already dusk by the time you reach the stairs."*
2. **A revelation.** *"You read the words: 'Keeper of the first light, do not wake.' Silence."*
3. **A line that lands.** An NPC says something that hangs in the air. Cut.
4. **An interruption.** Someone bursts in; the bell rings; the door opens.

End on the image. Cut to the next scene. **The instinct to cover every transition is the new-DM trap.** "And then they walked outside, and then the road was muddy, and then they reached..." Cut. Just cut.

> **[HOUSE] The 90-second post-decision rule.** Once players have made the decision the scene was *for*, you have 90 seconds of grace to handle small last-minute requests (*"can I grab one more torch?"*, *"I want to slip Bessa a coin before we leave"*) before you should hard-cut.
>
> Past 90 seconds, scenes drift into *"and another thing—"* territory. Cut firmly. *"You can do all of that. As you're doing it, the bell rings."*

### When to call the scene (cut triggers)

The DM has the camera. **If you don't cut, no one does.** Specific cut triggers — when any one of these fires, end the scene:

| Trigger | Action |
|---|---|
| A player says a line you couldn't have written | Cut on it. (You can return to the scene later if needed.) |
| The decision has been made; consequence is obvious | Cut to the consequence. |
| The conversation has gone in a circle for 2+ player-turns | Cut, even mid-sentence if you have to. |
| A clock you set is overdue to tick | Fire the clock; that's the cut. |
| A player has been silent for 10+ minutes | Cut to *them*. Make the next scene about them. |
| Real time is 30+ min over the scene's budget | Cut. Whatever clue was supposed to land here moves to the next scene. |

### The 30-min-over playbook

You're 30 minutes behind schedule. You know it. Players don't (yet). The playbook, in priority order:

1. **Stop allowing free-explore.** Switch to single-question prompts. *"What's your one priority before sunset?"* instead of *"What do you want to do now?"*
2. **Fire a clock.** Whatever clock is closest, ring it. The bell. The messenger. The patron's deadline. Ringing a clock is a sanctioned interruption.
3. **Skip transitional travel.** *"The walk to the smith takes ten minutes — you arrive. Tamsin is already turning to greet you."*
4. **Merge two scenes into one.** Tamsin is at the inn when the party returns; the smith conversation happens *there*. You just saved 20 minutes.
5. **Cut a scene entirely.** Pick the scene the players were least excited about. Its clue moves to the next-most-relevant NPC.
6. **If 45+ min over by Act III:** start the cliffhanger sequence early. The players don't know what was supposed to come next — they only know what landed.

Module 1 already has its drop-list ("What you can drop if you're short on time" in [`module-1-dm-flow.md`](../docs/dm-handbook/module-1-dm-flow.md)). **Every module should have its equivalent.** Build it during prep, not during play.

### The clock arsenal

Clocks are the DM's pacing power tool. Three useful types:

| Type | What it is | Use for |
|---|---|---|
| **Visible clock** (e.g. Day Clock in M1) | Players see it; you tick it openly | Time-of-day, deadlines players know about, public events |
| **Hidden clock** (e.g. Awakening Clock in M1) | Players don't know it exists; you tick it silently | Rising stakes, off-screen NPCs reacting, the world catching up |
| **Fuse** (one-shot) | "When X happens, Y fires." No ticking — single trigger | Traps with delayed effects, conditional alliances, threshold consequences |

> **[HOUSE] The clock-fire rule.** **If you set a clock, it MUST fire** at some point in the session, even if conditions don't strictly meet the trigger. A clock that never fires teaches players you don't keep your word about stakes — and after they've learned that lesson once, no clock works anymore.
>
> Better to fire it *gentler* than to never fire it at all. *"The wind doesn't quite shift to a storm — but you can taste the rain on it. Tonight is the night it would have."*

### In-game time tracking

Use [`docs/dm-handbook/session-time-tracker.md`](../docs/dm-handbook/session-time-tracker.md). It's already built. The core habits from that file:

- **Always know what time it is in the world.** Even if you don't tell players, *you* must know.
- **Update on scene change**, not every minute. The clock is a stage marker, not a stopwatch.
- **Costs to advance the in-game clock** (canonical table from the time tracker):

| Action | Time |
|---|---|
| One combat round | 6 sec |
| Don light armor | 1 min |
| Don medium armor | 5 min |
| Don heavy armor | 10 min |
| Searching a room thoroughly | 10 min (one dungeon turn) |
| Short rest | 1 hour |
| Long rest | 8 hours |
| Travel 1 mile (normal pace) | ~20 min |
| Travel 1 mile (fast pace) | ~15 min |
| Travel 1 mile (slow / stealthy) | ~30 min |
| Day's travel on foot | 24 miles (8 hours) |
| Day's travel on horseback | 32 miles |

The biggest in-game time mistake is **letting the day pass without anyone noticing.** If the party spends 6 in-game hours in the village without acknowledgment, the world feels frozen. **Drop one *"the sun is past midday now"* line per real-world hour.** It's how the players feel time moving.

### The narrative arc — the four-beat session

Every session of any module should hit these four beats:

| Beat | What | % of session |
|---|---|---|
| **ANCHOR** | Where we left off, who's in the scene, what's at stake. Re-establishes the world. | ~10% (~20 min) |
| **CHOICES** | Players have real decisions. Free-explore blocks, social pillar, exploration. | ~55% (~100 min) |
| **SPIKE** | The session's biggest moment. Combat, revelation, betrayal, escape. | ~25% (~50 min) |
| **LAND** | The cliffhanger. The cold close. The image they'll carry to next session. | ~10% (~20 min) |

The order is rarely strictly 1→2→3→4. You can spike in the middle and re-spike at the end. But every session needs **at least one of each**:

- Without an ANCHOR → players feel disconnected, like the campaign restarted.
- Without CHOICES → it feels like a movie they watched.
- Without a SPIKE → flat. Forgettable.
- Without a LAND → unfinished. Players go home wondering what was supposed to happen.

### How time, pacing, and the dial relate

This section ties back to §2:

- **Cutting a scene** = a Level-2 INTERVENE (you inserted what they didn't ask for: the bell, the messenger, the cut).
- **Asking more questions to expand** = staying at Level-0 NARRATE.
- **Firing a clock** = Level-2 INTERVENE, but a sanctioned one (the clock was earned).
- **30-min-over playbook step 6 (cliffhanger early)** = approaches Level-3 OVERRIDE territory if done badly; do it as INTERVENE (the world produces the cliffhanger; you don't break the contract).

When in doubt about whether a pacing move is okay, ask: *"Did the fiction produce this, or did my impatience produce this?"* If the fiction, fire. If your impatience, find a way to make the fiction produce it before you fire.

## 5 · Spotlight Management

§1 promised: *every player goes home having done something only their character could have done.* This section keeps that promise across 3-5 players and 3.5 hours.

### The math of attention

A 3.5-hour session is ~3 hours of play. With 5 players, that's **~36 minutes of spotlight each** if shared evenly. It's never even — and shouldn't be — but if one player has had 70 minutes and another has had 8, you have a problem you may not have noticed.

> **[HOUSE] The tally.** Keep a light tally on your DM sheet: a tick each time a player drives a scene or has a real moment. Glance at it at your session's midpoint (the Sunset Bell in M1). Whoever has the fewest ticks gets the next moment you can hand out.

### One scene, one lead

Most scenes have a natural **lead** — the player whose character the scene is *about*. Social scene with Bran → the face. Lock to pick → the rogue. Dawnweave inscription → the scholar.

Your job: **rotate the lead.** If the rogue led the last two scenes, design the next to spotlight someone else. The lead isn't the only one acting — they're the one the camera is on.

### The round-robin trick

The single most useful spotlight tool. When one player has been talking for 2-3 minutes:

> *"While [PC1] talks to Mordren — [PC2], what are you doing? Listening, or looking around the inn?"*

Three things at once: it cuts the monologue, hands the camera to PC2, and gives PC2 something concrete to react to.

### Tools for the quiet player

| Tool | How |
|---|---|
| **Direct address** | Don't wait for them to speak. *"Vesper, you notice something the others don't —"* |
| **The designed moment** | Plan ONE scene per session only their character can resolve. The doctor's patient. The soldier's old enemy. The scholar's inscription. |
| **The NPC who asks for them** | An NPC seeks out the quiet PC specifically. *"Garret won't talk to anyone but you."* |
| **Low-stakes prompts** | Quiet players often fear high-stakes spotlight. Start small: *"What does your character order at the bar?"* |

### Tools for the dominant player

Dominant players aren't a problem — they're fuel. Channel them:

| Tool | How |
|---|---|
| **The gentle redirect** | *"Love it. Let's hear what [quiet PC] thinks before you act."* |
| **A job that needs others** | *"You're leading the descent — who's behind you, and who's got the light?"* (forces them to involve the table) |
| **The yes-and reward** | When they set up another player, reward it (Heroic Inspiration, §3). Train the behavior you want. |
| **The private check-in** | Between sessions, one sentence: *"You're great at driving scenes — help me pull the quieter folks in?"* Most say yes happily. |

### Spotlight by pillar

Every session should touch all **three pillars** — combat, exploration, social. Different PCs shine in different pillars. The Bard owns social; the Rogue owns exploration; the Fighter owns combat. **A session that's all combat starves the Bard's player; all talk starves the Fighter's.**

> **[HOUSE] The pillar check.** In prep, ask: does next session have at least one real combat beat, one real exploration beat, one real social beat? If not, add the missing one — or you'll have a bored player.

### Your party's spotlight map

Your four PCs split cleanly:

- **Social:** Bard (Cha 15) leads; Stannis (Cha 14, Intimidation) backs.
- **Exploration:** Vesper (Rogue, stealth/traps) leads; Druid (Survival/Nature) backs.
- **Combat:** Stannis (Fighter) leads; everyone contributes.
- **Lore / The Notice:** currently *no one* has Lore (Dawnweave) — a gap. Either an NPC (Mordren, Sister Elra) carries it, or it's an arc for a player to grow into. Worth noticing.

Make each lead shine once per session.

### One-page summary

```

  ~36 min/player in a 5-person session (never even; watch the extremes)
  [HOUSE] Keep a tally; fewest ticks gets the next moment.

  ONE SCENE, ONE LEAD — rotate who the camera's on.
  ROUND-ROBIN: "While X does that, Y — what are you doing?"

  QUIET PLAYER:    direct address · designed moment · NPC asks for them · low stakes
  DOMINANT PLAYER: gentle redirect · job that needs others · reward setups · private chat
```

## 6 · Improv Framework

The "players did something I didn't plan for" pain point. Tools for buying thinking time, deciding fast, and turning surprises into gifts.

### The three-question stall

When a player declares an intention you didn't anticipate, you have ~5 seconds before silence reads as uncertainty. Use the three-question stall to **buy thinking time *while* extracting useful information** from the player:

1. **"What does that look like?"** — They describe the action. You get concrete specifics to rule on.
2. **"Why now?"** — Forces them to commit motivation. Often reveals the specific outcome they're hoping for.
3. **"Who's watching?"** — Forces them to consider audience. Bystanders, NPCs, allies. Changes the social weight of the action.

You don't need all three. One usually suffices. The point: **you're not stalling, you're investigating.** It looks like good DMing because it *is* good DMing.

### Default to YES (the §2 ladder, applied to surprises)

When players surprise you, default to the top rungs of the YES Ladder:

- **YES-AND** if it makes the fiction richer. *"Yes, and Bessa stops mid-knead when you say that. The whole inn quiets."*
- **YES-BUT** if reasonable with a price. *"Yes, but doing that in front of Bran means he'll remember it forever."*
- **YES-IF** if uncertain. *"Roll Persuasion DC 13."*

The instinct to say NO when surprised is a defense mechanism. **Override it.** Most surprises are gifts — players have invested attention and made a creative leap. Reward that.

### The retcon window

You make a ruling. 30 seconds later you realize you were wrong, or it had consequences you didn't see, or a player gently pushes back. You may retcon, within these limits:

| Window since ruling | Action |
|---|---|
| **< 30 seconds** | Retcon freely. *"Wait — let me take that back. DC 12, not 15."* No one will mind. |
| **30 sec to 5 min** | Retcon with acknowledgment. *"I want to walk that back. I called it wrong. Here's how it actually goes..."* Players respect this. |
| **> 5 min** | Don't retcon the ruling mid-session. Apply the lesson next time. Add to the ruling log (§7). |

> **[HOUSE] The retcon is a tool, not a habit.** If you retcon more than twice per session, you're under-thinking before ruling. Slow down by 5 seconds before each call.

### "I need a moment" is a fine sentence

When you don't know what to do, say so. Specific phrasings:

- *"I need a moment. Talk among yourselves for 30 seconds."*
- *"Hold on — let me think about how this resolves."*
- *"Good question. Give me ten seconds."*

**This is not weakness. This is craft.** Players respect a DM who thinks before ruling; they lose respect for a DM who rules wrong and refuses to fix it.

**The trap:** never say *"I need a moment"* and then talk for two minutes. Either think silently (players are fine with that) or actually use the moment. Don't ramble while thinking aloud.

### When players go off the script (or the map)

The most common improv pain is when players head somewhere or do something you don't have prep for. Three options, in priority order:

1. **Reskin existing material.** That unprepared NPC sounds suspiciously like Mordren. That unprepared location is just the inn with different furniture. **Reskinning is not cheating — it's reuse.**
2. **Pull prep forward.** A scene from later in the module happens earlier. The patron meets them at the inn instead of the cottage. The clue lives on a different NPC's lips.
3. **Take a fictional-time break.** *"It takes you two hours to reach the next village. Let's break for ten real minutes."* Use those ten minutes to sketch what they'll find.

> **[HOUSE] Pre-stocked NPCs.** Keep **2–3 stock NPCs ready** at all times — a name, one adjective, one tic, one secret. When players go somewhere you didn't prep, drop a stocked NPC and build the scene around them. After the session, write them up in your NPC tracker so they exist next time.
>
> Example stock NPCs for Fallowton-region travel:
> - **Marn the carter:** stout, suspicious, refuses to look you in the eye, secretly carrying smuggled letters.
> - **Sera Vox, traveling cleric:** thin, smiles too quickly, secretly excommunicated.
> - **Old Vell, retired soldier:** silent, drinks alone, secretly served under the grandfather of someone the party has met.

### The improv-to-canon pipeline

Things you improvise become campaign canon if they're used twice. The pipeline:

1. **At the table:** improvise. Don't break flow to write it down.
2. **End-of-session log (5 min):** write down any improvised name, place, fact, or ruling.
3. **Next session prep:** decide which improvised elements *deserve* canon status. Promote them in `wiki/` or `homebrew/world/`.

**Why it matters:** players track everything. If you said the moon is silver in Session 1 and gold in Session 5, **a player will remember and ask.** Track your improv.

### The "deflect to a player" move

When you're stuck and need 30 seconds *and* want to give a player a moment, deflect:

- *"[PC name], you know this region better than anyone. What's the closest village to the north?"*
- *"You've heard a story about something like this. Tell me one detail you remember."*
- *"Your character has met someone like this before. Who? What did they want?"*

**The player builds the world.** You take their answer as canon and move forward. This rewards engaged players and saves you from improvising blind.

> **[HOUSE] Use this once per session, max.** Twice starts to feel like you're outsourcing the world. Once feels like collaborative storytelling.

```

  You're not stalling — you're investigating.

  YES-AND > YES-BUT > YES-IF > NO-BECAUSE > NO
```

## 7 · Ruling on the Fly

Where §3 told you *when* to roll, §7 tells you what to do when a player tries something the rulebook doesn't cleanly cover. The "rules I had to guess at" pain point.

### The 30-second rule

When a rules question comes up and you don't know the answer:

1. **You have 30 seconds.** Make a ruling.
2. **Look up the rule between sessions**, not during.
3. **If you were wrong, change the ruling for next session.** Don't retcon mid-session unless it's egregious (per §6 retcon window).

**Why 30 seconds:** longer interrupts flow. Players checking the rulebook mid-session breaks immersion for everyone. **Better to be 80% right and fast than 100% right and slow.**

### The four-question fast-rule

When a player tries something the rulebook doesn't clearly cover, run this check in your head:

1. **What's the closest existing rule?** (Is this similar to a skill check, save, contest, or attack?)
2. **What's the in-fiction outcome of success?** (Pictureable. Concrete.)
3. **What's the in-fiction outcome of failure?** (Pictureable. Has consequence.)
4. **What's the DC, roughly?** (Use the fixed-rung ladder from §3: 10/13/15/18.)

If you can answer all four in 30 seconds, **rule it.** If you can't answer #2 or #3, the answer is probably *"don't roll, just say what happens"* (per §2/§3).

### The fairness test

Before you commit to a ruling, ask:

> *"Would I rule the same way if it was the player I like least, or for the NPC I think is most boring?"*

If yes → rule. If no → you're letting affection or aesthetics influence rules. That will erode table trust over time.

This applies to advantage grants, DC choices, partial successes, what counts as "in range" or "in line of sight." **Be the same DM for everyone.**

### Ruling-not-rule memory

When you make a ruling on the fly, **write it down.** Two reasons:

1. **Consistency.** Next time the same situation comes up, rule the same way. Players notice consistency. They especially notice inconsistency.
2. **The wiki gets richer.** Repeated rulings become house-rule canon, eventually documented in this manual or in `homebrew/`.

**At-the-table format:** sticky note, single line. *"Pushing through a corridor while holding a torch = Athletics DC 12, no torch dropped on success."* That's enough.

**Post-session format:** add to [`wiki/rulings/`](../wiki/) if it'll recur. The wiki already has a `rulings/` folder for exactly this purpose. Use the `ruling` type from `wiki/_templates/`.

### When to break your own rule

You will, occasionally, want to break a ruling you established earlier. **You may, but only when:**

1. **The previous ruling was wrong** (you've looked it up and realized you misruled). Announce the change. *"We've been ruling this wrong; here's the actual mechanic."* Apply going forward, not retroactively.
2. **The previous ruling produced an outcome you didn't anticipate** that now breaks the game. Announce and revise. Don't pretend it was always this way.
3. **The fiction changed enough that the ruling no longer applies.** *"Last time it was just three guards. Now there's a captain. Stealth DC bumps from 13 to 15."*

**You may NOT break your own rule** to:

- Make a planned encounter harder because the players are doing well.
- Soften a consequence because a player got upset.
- Apply a different rule to NPCs than to PCs without telling the table.

**The principle:** *rule-breaking is allowed when the world changes; it is not allowed when the DM wants a different outcome.*

### The "I'll look it up" promise

When you genuinely don't have time to rule in 30 seconds, say:

> *"I'll rule this as X for now. I'll look it up between sessions and we'll lock the rule next time."*

Then:

1. Write the X ruling down at the table.
2. Look up the actual rule before next session.
3. Open next session by announcing the locked rule: *"Last session we ruled X. The book actually says Y. We'll use Y from now on, starting from this moment forward."*

**Players love this.** It signals you take rules seriously without slowing the table.

### Common mid-session disputes — pre-decided rulings

These come up often enough to pre-decide. Reference this table at the table if needed.

| Situation | [HOUSE] Default ruling |
|---|---|
| "Can I use my reaction to do X?" | If X has a Reaction listed (Opportunity Attack, specific spells/features), yes. Otherwise: no, but you can use **Ready an Action** next round to set up the same effect. |
| "Can I drop my weapon as part of another action?" | Yes. Free object interaction. Once per turn. |
| "Can darkvision see through a window?" | Yes if the far side is dim light or darker; treats darkness as dim, dim as bright. |
| "Can I cast a spell while grappled?" | Yes if no Somatic component, or you have a free hand and the spell allows. The grapple doesn't break concentration unless you take damage. |
| "Can I move through an enemy's space?" | Only if they're Prone, Incapacitated, or two sizes different. Otherwise: difficult terrain into their space at best; often blocked. |
| "Can I take a long rest in the dungeon?" | RAW says yes if undisturbed 8 hrs. **[HOUSE]:** only in confirmed safe areas. The Awakening Clock continues to tick during rests. |
| "Nat 20 on a skill check — is that auto-success?" | **[HOUSE] YES** — see below. |
| "Can I attack and then move the rest of my speed?" | Yes. Movement is split-able across the turn freely as long as total ≤ speed. |
| "Can I help an ally and then attack on the same turn?" | No. Help is an Action; Attack is an Action. Use Bonus Action features (Bardic Inspiration, Healing Word) instead. |

> **[HOUSE] Natural 20 / Natural 1 on non-attack rolls.** BR-2024 doesn't grant auto-success on a natural 20 for skill checks (only attack rolls). We allow it:
>
> - **Nat 20:** succeeds *and* gives one extra benefit — a free piece of info, a free question answered, advantage on the next related check, or a small narrative gift.
> - **Nat 1:** fails *and* incurs one extra complication — a tool breaks, an NPC notices something off, a small narrative cost.
>
> Both treat the natural extreme as a **story event, not a math event.** Players love rolling for the chance.

### The ruling log

Maintain a single file at [`wiki/rulings/`](../wiki/) (already exists in the wiki structure). Append every ruling that you think will come up again.

Format (use `wiki/_templates/ruling.md` template):

```
---
type: ruling
question: "Can a PC use Help to grant advantage on a skill check from across the room?"
answered: 2026-05-23
---

# Help action across a room

**Question:** Can a PC use the Help action to grant advantage on a skill check from across the room, by shouting advice?

**Ruling:** No. Help requires being within 5 ft, OR being able to physically participate in the task.
For purely informational shouts (e.g. "the lock has a left-handed pin"), the DM may award a one-time +1
or +2 depending on relevance — but not full advantage.

**Source:** [BR-2024 §rules-glossary#HelpAction]

**Why we ruled this way:** Help is a physical-or-immediate-presence action. Advantage stacking from
shouted advice would let one Bard-with-Expertise carry every check.
```

## 8 · Anti-patterns

The "don't do this" list. Each is a trap new DMs fall into; each has a section of this manual that fixes it.

### At the table

| Anti-pattern | Why it's bad | Do instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Lecturing rules mid-scene** | Kills the fiction; bores the table | Rule it in 30 seconds, look it up later (§7) |
| **Punishing creativity with a harder DC** | Teaches players to stop being creative | Reward the idea — lower the DC or say YES-AND (§2) |
| **Rolling when failure has no story** | Wastes time; produces anticlimax | Don't roll; give them the thing (§3) |
| **"Are you sure?" as a tell** | Players learn it means "you're about to do something dumb" | Use it rarely; let them make mistakes |
| **Fudging dice against players** | Destroys trust permanently | Never. The dice are sacred (§2 Cardinal Rule 1) |
| **NPC monologues** | The spotlight leaves the players | Cut NPCs to 1-3 sentences; let players interrupt |
| **Letting one player monopolize** | Starves the others | Round-robin; rotate the lead (§5) |

### In prep

| Anti-pattern | Why it's bad | Do instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Over-prepping a plot** | Players deviate; forcing them back = railroad | Prep *situations*, not plots. NPCs and places, not scripted scenes (§10) |
| **Planning for 100% of session time** | You always overrun | Plan for 60% (§4) |
| **One path to a clue** | Miss the roll, the story stalls | Every clue reachable by 2+ paths |
| **A clock you won't fire** | Teaches players your stakes are fake | If you set a clock, it fires (§4) |

### In tone

| Anti-pattern | Why it's bad | Do instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Every NPC suspicious/hostile** | The world feels flat and paranoid | Most people are just people. Save menace for when it matters |
| **Info-dumping lore** | Players glaze over | Reveal lore through play — an object, a remark, a roll |
| **NPCs who outshine the PCs** | The PCs stop feeling like protagonists | NPCs serve the PCs' story. Sister Elra is wise, but the PCs act |
| **Relentless grimness** | The campaign becomes oppressive | Make room for Joy (`sanity-and-spirit.md` §H6.4). Let the village have a good day |
| **Forgetting last session** | Players feel their choices don't matter | Keep the player-choice log; open with a callback (§10) |

### The Fallowton-specific anti-patterns

- **Don't let the homebrew systems run the table.** Wounds, Notice, Strain, tolerance — seasoning, not the meal. If you spend more time on bookkeeping than story, cut bookkeeping.
- **Don't make Sister Elra a vending machine.** She's a person with a burden of 5, not a heal-dispenser. Spread restoration roles across NPCs.
- **Don't tick The Notice for everything.** It's the campaign's central horror; if it ticks every scene, it stops being scary. Tick it for *meaningful* Hollow Star contact, not ambiance.
- **Don't forget Joy.** The grim systems are load-bearing, but a campaign that only punishes burns players out. Schedule a Barleynight. Let a romance land. Give the cleric a session off.

### One-page summary

```
ANTI-PATTERNS — don't:
  TABLE:  lecture rules · punish creativity · roll w/o stakes · "are you sure?" tell ·
          fudge vs players · NPC monologue · let one player dominate
  PREP:   over-prep plot · plan 100% · single clue path · unfired clock
  TONE:   all NPCs hostile · info-dump lore · NPCs outshine PCs ·
          relentless grim · forget last session
  FALLOWTON: homebrew runs the table · Elra as vending machine ·
             Notice ticks for everything · forgetting Joy
```

## 9 · Session Shape

§4 introduced the four-beat arc (Anchor → Choices → Spike → Land). This is the full ritual of running a session start to finish.

### The cold open (first 5 minutes)

1. **Recap.** One minute. *"Last time, you descended, peeked all three tunnel mouths, and Mordren vanished overnight. You're back on the surface; it's dawn."* Or ask a player to recap — engages them, reveals what they remember.
2. **Re-establish the scene.** Where you are, who's here, the immediate situation.
3. **The hook.** End the cold open with a thing happening. *"Bran is standing at the sinkhole stairs holding a letter with a royal seal. What do you do?"*

### The four beats (detailed)

| Beat | Duration | What you're doing | Working when... |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ANCHOR** | ~10% | Recap + re-establish + hook | Players remember where they were and care about the stakes |
| **CHOICES** | ~55% | Free play; players drive; you react | Players make decisions you didn't fully predict |
| **SPIKE** | ~25% | The session's biggest moment — combat, betrayal, revelation, escape | The table goes quiet or loud; phones are down |
| **LAND** | ~10% | The cliffhanger or cold close; the image they carry home | Players protest that you're stopping |

The order flexes — spike early, re-spike at the end — but every session needs all four.

### The mid-session pulse check

At the midpoint (M1: the Sunset Bell), check three gauges:

- **Time:** on pace? (If 30+ min over, run the §4 over-budget playbook.)
- **Tone:** is the table feeling the tone you want? Gone goofy and you want dread → plant a quiet dread beat. Gone grim and players flagging → plant a Joy beat.
- **Spotlight:** glance at your tally (§5). Who hasn't had a moment? Plan one for the back half.

### The cliffhanger ritual (how to end)

You'll *want* to keep going after the climax. Don't.

1. **Deliver the landing image** — Mordren's empty chair; the silver leaving the well; the forgotten name.
2. **Say the words: "We end there."**
3. **Close the screen / put the notes down.** Physically signal the end.
4. **Let them protest.** Protest = a built-in audience for next session.

Then immediately do the **5-minute after-session debrief** (§10) while it's fresh.

### Session length management

- **3.5 hours is the target.** Past 4, attention collapses regardless of content quality.
- **One break** at the midpoint (the pulse check is a natural spot). 10 minutes.
- **Running long? Cut from CHOICES, never from LAND.** Players forgive a shorter free-play; they never forgive a rushed cliffhanger.

### One-page summary

```
SESSION SHAPE
  COLD OPEN (5 min): recap · re-establish · hook
  ANCHOR (10%) → CHOICES (55%) → SPIKE (25%) → LAND (10%)
  MID-SESSION PULSE: time? · tone? · spotlight?
  CLIFFHANGER RITUAL: landing image → "We end there." → close screen → let them protest
  RUN LONG? cut from CHOICES, never LAND. One break at midpoint. Hard stop by 4 hrs.
```

## 10 · Between-Sessions Ritual

What you do between sessions decides whether the campaign compounds or drifts. The ritual: ~5 minutes right after, ~60 minutes before next time.

### Immediately after (5 minutes, while fresh)

Three debrief questions, asked of yourself:

1. **Who at the table was quietest?** Plan a moment for them next session.
2. **Which clue/beat did the party miss that you were excited about?** Move it to a different NPC's lips — clues aren't consumed by going unfound.
3. **Which moment, in your gut, did the table go still?** That's the tone of your campaign. Lean into it.

Plus the mechanical closeout (per [`../docs/dm-handbook/module-1-integration.md`](../docs/dm-handbook/module-1-integration.md) for M1): update each PC's Notice, Strain, Wounds; note Sister Elra's burden; **write down any player-narrated Visions — they're canon now.**

### Before next session (60 minutes: 30 review + 30 plan)

**Review (30 min):**
- Read your session log + player-choice log. What did they do? Who did they like, bribe, anger?
- Update the long-term trackers (below).
- Look up any rules you ruled on the fly (§7) and lock them.
- Review each player's level-up **Story Beats** (Recover/Study/Contact/Pay/Witness, per `character-design/progression.md`) — each is a promise; plan to pay it.

**Plan (30 min):**
- **The anchor:** how does next session open? What's the hook?
- **The choices:** what 3-5 *situations / locations / NPCs* will you offer? (Situations, not scripted scenes.)
- **The spike:** the biggest beat you're aiming for? (Be ready to let it move.)
- **The land:** the cliffhanger or image you hope to end on? (Have a backup.)
- **The pillar check:** combat + exploration + social all present? (§5)
- **Plan for 60%** of the time (§4). The other 40% is theirs.

### The long-term trackers (your campaign bible)

| Tracker | What it holds |
|---|---|
| **Player-choice log** | What the party did each session; who they helped/harmed; what they said they'd do |
| **NPC relationship map** | Each named NPC's standing toward the party (+/−/?); what they remember |
| **World Notice** | The Fallowton ruin's current Notice; other sites as they emerge (`the-notice.md`) |
| **Per-PC Notice / Strain / Wounds** | Carried across sessions |
| **Faction standing** | Concord, the Office, hedge folk, village |
| **Campaign clocks** | Deadlines and rising threats (the Awakening, the Office's Walk, brewing conflicts) |
| **Seed threads** | The W.V. dagger; Mordren's patron; the Voss line; whatever the players latched onto |
| **Improvised canon** | Names/places/facts you invented at the table (§6 — promote them so they persist) |

### The compounding principle

The reason for all of this: **players track everything, so you must too.** A campaign feels alive when the world *remembers* — when the NPC you were rude to in session 2 is cold in session 7; when the clue you missed resurfaces; when the level-up Story Beat from session 4 pays off in session 9. That memory is the difference between a series of disconnected sessions and a *campaign*.

> **[HOUSE] The one-file campaign bible.** Keep all of the above in a single living file (or your existing tracking sheets in [`../dmg-tracking-sheets/`](../dmg-tracking-sheets/)). Update it in the 5-minute after-debrief and the 30-min review. It's the most valuable prep you do.

### One-page summary

```
BETWEEN-SESSIONS RITUAL
  AFTER (5 min, fresh):
    1. who was quietest? (plan their moment)
    2. what clue was missed? (move it to a new NPC)
    3. when did the table go still? (that's your tone)
    + mechanical closeout (Notice / Strain / Wounds / Visions)

  BEFORE (60 min = 30 review + 30 plan):
    REVIEW: logs · trackers · locked rulings · player Story Beats
    PLAN:   anchor · choices (situations not scripts) · spike · land
            pillar check · plan for 60%

  CAMPAIGN BIBLE: choice log · NPC map · World Notice ·
    per-PC Notice/Strain/Wounds · faction standing · clocks ·
    seed threads · improvised canon

  THE WORLD REMEMBERS, SO THE DM MUST.
```
