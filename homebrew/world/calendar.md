# The Calendar of the Sealed Age

The clock Fallowton runs on. Farmers don't quote the date — they quote the work. *"Past the third Sownday."* *"Two weeks before threshing."* The calendar exists for tax men, priests, and scholars. Everyone else lives by barley.

> See also: [[weave-strain-magic]] (Dawnweave / Sealing timeline), [[background-sinkhole-witness]] (Fallowton agriculture).

## Year structure

- **12 months × 30 days = 360 days.**
- **5 intercalary days** at the year's hinge, called the **Sealing Days** — outside any month, "between the year and itself." Total: **365 days.**
- **Leap rule:** every fourth year adds a sixth Sealing Day, called the **Long Hush.** People are supposed to keep silent from dusk to dusk. They don't.
- **6-day week × 5 weeks per month.** Days are named: **Mornsday, Plowday, Midweek, Hearthday, Marketday, Restday.** Marketday is when Fallowton's barn-yard fills with carts from outlying farms. Restday is observed in name only — gray wheat doesn't care.

**Why this shape:** the year is older than the kingdom. The crown standardized the count after the Sealing because tax assessments depended on it. The five Sealing Days were inserted to "make the books balance" — the official story. The actual reason is that the Dawnweave year was 365 and a fraction, the modern year is 360 plus the five, and nobody who could explain the difference is alive.

## Months

The order, with seasons. Names that sound ordinary often carry Dawnweave origins — most modern speakers don't notice, the way nobody hears *Augustus* when they say *August*.

| # | Month | Season | Hidden origin (DM-only unless rolled) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Hollowmonth** | deep winter | "Hallowing-month" — when Converters were drained and re-anointed |
| 2 | **Wether** | late winter | "Wethering" — Dawnweave term for unsealing a chamber; villagers think it means *wet weather* |
| 3 | **Greenstir** | early spring | "Greening-stair" — Converter staircases were said to flower with first light |
| 4 | **Sownmonth** | mid-spring | sounds folk; actually "Soun-moon," a Dawnweave architect's name |
| 5 | **Brightleaf** | late spring | "Bright-Leaf" was the title of the first priest-converter |
| 6 | **Highsun** | early summer | the one month whose name means exactly what it says |
| 7 | **Reaph** | mid-summer | the architect Reaph designed the Houses of Light; "reaping" is folk-etymology |
| 8 | **Goldfall** | late summer | "Golden Fall" — the commissioning rite of the first Converter |
| 9 | **Threshmonth** | early autumn | genuinely about threshing; one of the few honest names |
| 10 | **Lampset** | mid-autumn | "Lamp-set" was a Dawnweave funerary lighting; nothing to do with autumn lamps |
| 11 | **Stillmonth** | late autumn | "Still" meant *unmoving,* as in the sealed core; villagers hear *quiet weather* |
| 12 | **Longnight** | early winter | the only month with an obvious meaning, and the one most people get wrong (it's not the longest night, that's a single day in *Hollowmonth*) |

**Sealing Days** (between Longnight 30 and Hollowmonth 1): named **First Hush, Second Hush, Third Hush, Fourth Hush, Fifth Hush.** Leap years add a **Long Hush** before First. The crown calls them the *Days of Order;* nobody else does.

## Seasons

| Season | Months | What Fallowton looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **Winter** | Longnight, Hollowmonth, Wether | gray sky, hard ground, gray wheat under snow if there is snow, smoke from every chimney |
| **Spring** | Greenstir, Sownmonth, Brightleaf | mud, rain, plowing, sowing, the wet smell that gets into the wool |
| **Summer** | Highsun, Reaph, Goldfall | bright, dry by late Reaph, the fields golden by Goldfall's end |
| **Autumn** | Threshmonth, Lampset, Stillmonth | sheaves stacked, turnip-tops above ground, the smell of woodsmoke and rotting leaves |

## Agricultural rhythm

The clock the village actually keeps.

- **Gray wheat** (the hardy winter cereal): sown **Lampset–Stillmonth** (autumn). Greens through winter under snow. Reaped late **Brightleaf** or early **Highsun** — first harvest of the year.
- **Barley** (the main crop, the one Fallowton's economy turns on): sown **Sownmonth** (the month is named for it). Knee-high by mid-**Brightleaf**, eared by **Reaph**, reaped **Goldfall**. Threshed **Threshmonth**.
- **Turnips** (the hedge against a bad barley year): sown end of **Reaph** through early **Goldfall** — a *second crop* in the same year, after the gray wheat ground is freed. Pulled **Stillmonth** through **Longnight** as needed.
- **Fallow rotation:** one field in three lies fallow each year. The north field — the one with the sinkhole — was due to lie fallow this year, which is why nobody was watching it closely when the ground gave.
- **Lambing:** late **Wether** into **Greenstir.**

A Fallowton farmer can tell you what month it is by walking the fields blindfolded. Barley knee-high = mid-Brightleaf. Wheat heads bowing = late Brightleaf. Air smelling of cut straw = Goldfall.

## Holidays

Four named holidays. Two crown, one folk, one Dawnweave remnant.

### Crown — the Crowning (Sownmonth 14)

State holiday. The day the first sealed-age monarch was crowned, **247 years ago** this Sownmonth. Magistrates read a proclamation from the inn steps. The crown's colors (deep blue and gold) hang from the smithy and the granary; nobody hangs them from their own house unless they want a magistrate's notice. Sister Elra leads a short blessing. In Fallowton it is observed because not observing it is noticed.

### Crown — the Office's Walk (Stillmonth 1)

The day the **Office of Sealed Practice** (see [[crown-and-office]], unbuilt) formally rides through each provincial village to "inspect for the season." Four riders. Sealed warrants. Nothing happens, usually. Children are kept indoors. Every household with a known caster has a quiet bag packed by the door. The walk takes an hour; the fear takes a week.

### Folk — Barleynight (Goldfall 27)

The night the last barley sheaf is bound. The youngest unmarried person in the village ties the final knot; the oldest cuts it. Everyone drinks the new beer, which is bad because it's young, and dances badly because the beer is bad. Fallowton's actual high holy day. A girl who kisses a boy on Barleynight isn't promising anything except that night.

### Dawnweave remnant — the Lampset Vigil (Lampset 12)

People in Fallowton light **a single candle in the western window** from dusk until midnight on Lampset 12 and don't speak of it during the day. If you ask why, they shrug. *"It's just what's done."* Sister Elra knows the prayer that goes with it but won't tell anyone who hasn't asked twice.

> **DM truth (History DC 15 / Religion DC 18 unlocks):** the Lampset Vigil commemorates the night the first Converter was **commissioned,** not sealed — the candles are an unconscious echo of the lamps that were lit along the House of First Light's western corridor during its anointing. Fallowton has been celebrating the founding of the machine buried under its own north field for at least eight generations, with nobody alive who remembers it.

### Dawnweave remnant — the Long Hush (leap-year sixth Sealing Day)

Every fourth year, an extra day inserted between Longnight and Hollowmonth. People are *supposed* to keep silent from dusk to dusk. Old people do. Young people don't. Travelers find villages where nobody will sell them bread until the sun sets.

> **DM truth (History DC 18):** the Long Hush was the day the gods finished sealing the world-magic core. The silence was originally so the sealing wards could hear themselves close.

## The current date — Module 1 opens

> **Greenstir 22, Year 247 of the Sealed Age.** A **Plowday.**

Late spring. The third week of Greenstir — barley has been in the ground a week, the gray wheat is greening fast in the south field, the lambs are out. It has rained for **eighteen days straight.** This morning, just before dawn, the rain stopped. The north field — fallow this year, supposed to lie quiet — has a hole in it the size of a barn.

Sister Elra was already awake when it happened. She does not say why.

## DM use — how to drop the calendar without lecturing

The calendar is a texture, not a quiz. Use it the way the village uses it: in passing, attached to work. Examples a DM can drop verbatim:

- *"It's the third week of Greenstir — the barley should be knee-high by Brightleaf, but with eighteen days of rain it's barely up. The whole village is behind."*
- *"Goodwife Bessa: 'Barleynight's still four months off, but if the north field stays under water we won't be drinking new beer this year.'"*
- *"Henric (apprentice smith): 'It's a Plowday. I should be at the forge. Da'll have my hide.'"*
- *"You pass the granary on your way to the inn — the crown's blue-and-gold is hanging from the gable. Crowning was eight days ago. Nobody's taken the colors down yet because nobody wants to be seen taking them down first."*
- *"Sister Elra, in passing: 'Lampset Vigil's a long way off. I don't know why I'm thinking about candles.'"* (drop only if the party has rolled History/Religion well — let the line *mean* something)
- *"The Office's Walk is in Stillmonth. Six months. Plenty of time for whatever this is to be over by then. Says Goodman Tamsin, who does not look like he believes it."*

**Don't:** announce the date at session open. **Do:** let the village's work tell time. The barley is the clock. The candles in the windows are the clock. The riders in Stillmonth are the clock.

When a player asks *"what day is it?"* — give them the in-fiction answer a villager would give. *"Plowday. Third week of Greenstir. Past sowing, before the gray wheat heads."* The number is for the DM. The work is for the table.
