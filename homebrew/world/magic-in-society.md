# Magic in Society

The texture of living in a world where magic is real and expensive. This is the **world-feel** companion to the mechanics in [`../../docs/campaign/weave-strain-magic.md`](../../docs/campaign/weave-strain-magic.md). The strain rules tell you what casting *costs*. This file tells you what a village thinks of the woman who paid it.

> See also: [[crown-and-office]] (the licensing apparatus), [[pantheon]] (sealed clergy), [[geography]] (cleansed springs, ruin lands), [[languages]] (the tongues casters know and the ones nobody should), [[calendar]] (Lampset Vigil, Sealing Days, the Office's Walk).

## 1 · The premise, in plain words

Magic is real. Magic is dear. The bill is paid by the caster, often by the people standing too close, and — for the bigger workings — by the road, the year, the unborn lamb.

Two hundred and forty-seven years after the gods sealed the world-magic core, every spell anyone casts is a withdrawal through a wound. Most people never see one cast on purpose. When they do, they remember the day. They remember the smell — wet iron and old smoke, or burnt wool, or just *cold* — and they remember the look on the caster's face after, which is the look of someone who has just paid for something they cannot return. Villagers don't say *magic*. They say **black craft** when afraid, **old work** when angry, **wrong tongue** when they want it to stop. That language is not ignorance. It is the memory of paying someone else's debt.

## 2 · The economics of magic

### Who pays casters

- **Nobility** pay licensed wizards by **annual retainer** — typically 40–80 crown sovereigns a year for a household wizard with the writ ([[crown-and-office]]) to operate inside the noble's estates. The wizard handles wards, message-stones, the occasional healing, and the social duty of standing behind a chair at feasts so the lord looks substantial.
- **The crown itself** pays the Office of Sealed Practice's sensitives and their college tutors. State scholarship for promising commoners exists on paper; in practice the entry exams require literacy in Office-Hand and Liturgical Calendar Script ([[languages]]), which is to say they require having grown up with money.
- **The Concord of the Sealed Light** pays its village clergy in board, vestments, and the small parish tithe. Sister Elra eats because Goodwife Bessa feeds her.
- **Towns and magistrates** post fee schedules at the Sealed Court in [[geography|Calder Vault]] — fixed price for fixed spells, with a surcharge for "field call."
- **Everyone else** barters. A hedge-cunning in the Bracken Downs takes a hen for a charm. A village midwife is paid in firewood by every household whose child she helped pull. Favors stretch over years.

### Typical prices (Calder Vault, licensed)

These are the posted fees at a regional Sealed Court. Compare a Fallowton fieldhand wage: **3 copper a day**. A commoner family clears maybe 6 silver a *month* after rent.

| Service | Licensed (Calder Vault) | Black-market / hedge | What it would feel like to pay |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cure Wounds** (1st-level, on the day) | **2 silver** | 6–8 copper + a hen | Two weeks' wages. A family that asks for it does not also eat meat that month. |
| **Detect Magic** (a single sweep of one room) | **5 copper** | a meal and a story | An afternoon's lost work. Mostly used by merchants checking goods. |
| **Identify** (one item, one hour) | **8 silver flat, plus 1 silver per "rarity-band" if the item is uncommon+** | varies wildly; 1–3 silver and the hedge keeps a copy of whatever they learn | More than a month's keep. Done once in a life, for the heirloom you weren't sure was real. |
| **Healing Word** (battlefield, urgent) | **1 silver** if you're lucky enough to be near one | nothing — there is no hedge-witch on a battlefield | The price the dying pay. |
| **Mending** (cracked plough; broken cup) | **2 copper** | a beer | Affordable. The one spell a village ever actually buys. |
| **Sending** (a single message to a known person) | **3 silver per attempt** | not available | What you pay when somebody might already be dead. |
| **Ritual hexes, charms, lucky strings** (no real magic in 90% of cases) | not offered | 1–3 copper | Bought constantly. Mostly placebo. Occasionally not. |

A licensed casting always comes with the wizard's **stamp** — a wax disc with the writ-holder's mark and the date. The stamp is the receipt and the legal cover. Possession of a stamp protects the *recipient* in court the way a doctor's note protects a soldier. The black-market caster issues no stamp. The black-market patient prays.

### The black market

Real, common, quiet. A field-hand bitten by something in the Mereswood does not ride four days to [[geography|Calder Vault]] for a Cure Wounds; they walk an hour to the granny in the next hamlet, who knows enough. Rural prices run 30–70% below the posted licensed rate, often paid in goods. Risk: the granny's casting is **unsealed practice** ([[crown-and-office]]), and if a sensitive's pendulum ([[crown-and-office]]) catches the residue within a fortnight, both the caster and the patient may be detained for questioning. Most squads do not push hard if the patient is dying and the caster is grey-haired. Some do.

### Magic items as currency

A magic item — *real* magic, not a hedge charm — is among the most valuable objects a commoner could ever hold. The average villager has never touched one. The objects move along three roads: **inheritance** (grandfather's silvered knife, no one quite knows what it does), **grave goods** (recovered from a Wealdmark keep by someone who survived enough Wealdmark to bring it home), and **state issue** (Office sensitives' pendulums, college-loaned focus stones, the lanterns the Concord lights at certain shrines). Items are **hoarded**, **mistrusted**, and often **buried again** by the family that inherits them. *"What did it cost to make this?"* is the standard question. Nobody answers it.

## 3 · The six castes of caster

Every person in the realm who pulls magic through the broken channel falls into one of these six. The lines between are policed mostly by the Office and mostly with violence.

### Crown-licensed wizards [LAWFUL · VISIBLE]

Rare. Maybe **two hundred** in the entire Sealed Realm. They hold the **writ of practice** from the [[crown-and-office|Office of Sealed Practice]], pay annual dues to their licensed college, live in cities or noble estates, and wear robes the cut of which signals their rank to anyone who knows. They cast openly, file quarterly logs of their workings (real ones, mostly), and are *colleagues* of the Office until they are *targets* of it. **Mordren Vale** is the closest thing Fallowton has ever seen — and he is unusual: a wizard sent by an unnamed royal office, with a writ nobody at the inn can read, who came to a village specifically because that is not where wizards go. The village tolerates him. They do not invite him to weddings.

### Sealed clergy [LAWFUL · VISIBLE]

The Concord of the Sealed Light's priests, channeling through prayer, focus-symbols, and (for the village line) bound prayer-books in Old Common ([[languages]]). Their casting is *divine practice* and licensed under the Concord's separate charter — a sister-document to the wizards' writ, slightly older, slightly more lenient. **Sister Elra** is licensed to invoke for healing, blessing, light, and the rites for the dead ([[pantheon]]). She files no quarterly log. She does file an annual one with the bishop at Saint Veyne's in [[geography|Marrowford]], handwritten, brief. Sealed clergy are tolerated because *faith is licensed too*, and a kingdom that bans priests cannot tax their parishioners.

### Village hedge-folk [GREY · MOSTLY-INVISIBLE]

Granny-cunning women, midwives who set teeth and bones with one hand and a charm with the other, charm-makers, peat-witches in the Cinderfen ([[geography]]), the smith's wife in Reach-Cant country who knows the right rhyme over a coal-fire. Technically every one of them is in violation of the writ. Practically the rural districts look the other way for two reasons: the village would die without them, and the Office prefers to pick fights it can win cleanly in front of a magistrate. A hedge-witch's casting is *small* — a 1st-level spell once a fortnight, a cantrip a day. Their strain ([[../../docs/campaign/weave-strain-magic.md]]) catches up with them slowly. Most are wrecked by sixty. None apply for the writ. The writ would require literacy, fees they cannot pay, and a four-day ride to a city they have never seen.

### The Office's sensitives [LAWFUL · INSTRUMENTALIZED]

State-sponsored magical talent. Plucked from their families as children — sometimes with the family's consent, sometimes with a sealed warrant — and raised at the licensed colleges. They cast little themselves; they are trained to **find**. The pendulum is their instrument, the **detection-cantrip** their workhorse, and the four-person squad ([[crown-and-office]]) their cage. They die younger than wizards. **Ysmae Tann**, the Hollowmarch sensitive ([[crown-and-office]]), is one — she was unsealed-born, smuggled into the college by a mother who died of the smuggling. She is twenty-eight and walks like a woman ten years older.

### Bloodline casters [GREY · OFTEN-HIDDEN]

Sorcerers and innate-magic species. Magic in the blood, often catalyzed at adolescence by terror, illness, or the death of a relative. Bloodline casters are the kind the crown has the hardest time loving: their power is not licensed because no one issued it; the family is suspect because the family is suspect; the village around them is suspect because the village around them did not turn the child in. Most bloodline casters live their first ten useful years **hidden** — taught to suppress, to not flinch, to tell certain stories about why the cat keeps dying — and reveal only when puberty makes suppression impossible. The careful ones present to the Office voluntarily and accept "evaluation" at a licensed college; the unlucky ones are pulled out of their homes by a four-person squad in the night. The **Voss line** (see §8) is one of Fallowton's quietly-rumored bloodlines.

### Outlaw casters [HUNTED · INVISIBLE-OR-DEAD]

The most dangerous category, and the one that contains the most pitiable people. Three flavors mix here: **radicals** (apprentices who refused the licensing exam on principle and went north into the woods; Halsten Crebb's son is one — [[crown-and-office]]); **the desperate** (people who learned to cast because their child was dying and the licensed wizard would not come without a sovereign in advance); and **the warped** (those who took warlock pacts in places the gods cannot hear, who eat at altars under the Wealdmark hills). The Office hunts all three with the same warrants and writes them up in the same paperwork. Villagers fear them more than the licensed wizards because the licensed wizards collect debts on a schedule.

## 4 · The personal toll across a life

What it looks like to have been a working caster for twenty years, then forty. The strain rules ([[../../docs/campaign/weave-strain-magic.md]]) describe what one spell costs. This is what *many* spells cost, over a life.

**The marks** (what you'd see across a tavern). Grey at the temples too early — a wizard of thirty-five is silvered like a man of fifty. A pallor that doesn't go away with sun. **Fingertips that don't warm up**, even by a fire, even holding a cup of tea. A faint sweet-cold smell on their breath in winter. The eyes go pale at the iris's edge — not white, just *thinner*, as if the colour has been used up around the rim. Some old wizards stop blinking quite right. Some stop reacting to **certain noises** — the high ring of a struck bell, the cry of a hawk overhead, the sound of someone calling their childhood name across a crowd. The channel takes those frequencies first and they do not come back.

**The habits of restraint.** An old caster does not gesture broadly. They walk through a room with their hands close to their body — not because they distrust the room, but because they have spent forty years not casting *by mistake*. They count breaths before they speak. They will not pick up an unfamiliar object until they have looked at it for a moment longer than seems polite. At table, they thank the cook before the host. They do not curse aloud. They eat slowly.

**What gets paid.** Memory comes in pieces and never the pieces you would have chosen. A wizard at sixty may not remember **a child's voice** — only that they had a child, only that the voice was warm. The **smell of a particular spring** — gone; the spring is still there, but the way it smelled in 219 SA is paid. **A friend's face.** The faces go first, then the names, then sometimes the fact that the friend existed at all. (This is the Converter sin in slow miniature: the channel still takes living memory; the seal has not made it gentle, only smaller.) Some old wizards keep **letters to themselves** in their breast-pocket — *your wife's name is Maerla, she has a freckle on her left cheek, you love her* — for the day they cannot answer the question without help.

**The line.** Villagers say *"wizards always collect debts."* The doc explains: so do the years. A wizard at thirty has a debt-book. A wizard at sixty *is* the debt-book.

## 5 · The geography of magic

Where the channel runs gentler and where it bites. See [[geography]] for the locations themselves.

### Safer ground

- **Cleansed springs.** A handful exist in the Reach. Water that has been blessed across three Concord seasons by the local priest; the village shrine at Fallowton has one in a small stone basin behind the chapel. Casting *near* a cleansed spring costs the strain it would always cost, but the **recovery** is faster (per the Weave Strain doc, story-recovery −2 or clear). Pilgrimages exist. Some are long.
- **Sanctified ground.** Concord shrines, blessed chapels, the inside of any building consecrated by the Concord and re-blessed at the **Lampset Vigil** ([[calendar]]). The chapel at Fallowton qualifies. The Bent Plow's common room does not, but the tiny shrine room above the kitchen does — Bessa keeps Vassa's lamp there.
- **Licensed crown chapels.** Built to the same plan in every city, with a cleansed font and a focus-stone embedded in the altar. Office sensitives may rest in them.
- **Pilgrimage sites.** Three known in the realm; one (the Saint's Pool near Carrowfen) is the destination of broken-strain pilgrims who can afford the journey and the hostel.

### More dangerous ground

- **Converter ruins.** [[../../docs/campaign/weave-strain-magic.md|The House of First Light]] under Fallowton's north field is the example the campaign is built on, but it has cousins — the **Drowned Mill** in [[geography|Cinderfen]], the **Black Adit** at Banthorp ([[geography]]). Inside any Converter chamber, strain bites harder, Overreach triggers at strain 2+, and the ruin "listens" — accrues attention as a clock the DM may tick. The mechanics live in the strain doc; the *feel* is that the room is interested in you.
- **The Wealdmark.** The old crown country west past Mereswood ([[geography]]). Strain is not modified by being there; the danger is different. The Wealdmark is full of pre-Sealing residue, old wards still half-functional, things that fed on the Sealing's aftermath and are still hungry. Cast in the Wealdmark and you may be answered.
- **Sites of old Sealing events.** Specific locations where the gods' sealing operations were performed. None are mapped on any map a PC will see. They feel **wrong** in the body before they feel wrong to the senses. Casting on one may produce strain costs the DM rolls for rather than the player chooses.
- **The bleed-zones.** Small patches — a single field, a hedge-corner, a span of road — where the broken channel runs unusually close to the surface. Casting there is *easier* and *worse*: the strain dice drop a step, but the surroundings remember every cast for a generation. The bleed-zones are how stories get started about haunted ground.

### Cosmic geography

Beneath all of it: the **world-magic core** (sealed; reachable in legend only), the **broken channel** that every cast pulls through (everywhere, equally, mostly), and the rare **bleed-zones** where the channel surfaces. The seal is what makes any of this survivable. The gods paid for the seal. Most of what they were is on the other side of it ([[pantheon]]).

## 6 · Magic items in this world

### Rarity, by tier

- **Common-rarity magic items:** essentially absent at the village level. A licensed wizard might own one; a soldier might be issued one. A villager will go a lifetime without touching one.
- **Uncommon:** found as grave goods or family heirlooms. Often unidentified — *"grandmother's locket, it gets warm sometimes"* — because the family cannot afford the Identify cost (§2).
- **Rare and above:** Dawnweave-era. Almost never in private hands. The Concord keeps a small cabinet of them in the [[geography|Calder Vault]] cathedral undercroft. The crown keeps a larger one in Highmoor. Many remain buried with their original owners in the Wealdmark.

### Trust and the question

The standard reaction to a stranger producing a magic item is **suspicion**, not awe. Any item powerful enough to be obviously magical was also expensive enough to make that someone paid. A villager who is shown a healing token will often ask, before accepting healing, *"what did it cost to make this?"* The honest answer is rarely good.

### Categories

| Category | What it is | How villagers feel about it |
|---|---|---|
| **Dawnweave artifacts** | Pre-Sealing, made on the Converter principle. Healing-strong, memory-cheap. | Terrified. Will not touch. Will not let a child near. |
| **Concord-blessed items** | Modern. Made by clergy in licensed sanctification rites. Lamps, healing pendants, ward-stones. | Respected. Trusted because the Concord vouched. Costly. |
| **Crown-stamped items** | Made by licensed wizards under college oversight. Focus rods, message-stones, sealed compasses. | Mistrusted but lawful. Often issued, rarely owned. |
| **Folk charms** | Hedge-made, ribbon, hawthorn, salt, a pebble from the right field. 90% placebo. | Universal. Tied above every door in the village. |

### The licensing of possession

For any item the Office classifies as **uncommon or higher**, the holder must carry a **permit of possession** issued by the Sealed Court at the duchy capital. The permit lists item, holder, date, and the chain of acquisition. Possession without permit is a charge equal to unsealed practice itself. Most uncommon items in commoner hands are **unpermitted**, **inherited**, and **hidden under the floor**. The Office knows this and does not push it unless a sensitive's pendulum catches the item active.

## 7 · The lived experience of not casting

Most people in the realm will never cast a spell. Their relationship to magic is **observation and avoidance.**

**Children's stories** in Fallowton are full of warnings: the boy who said the word and could not stop saying it; the girl whose cat got smaller every time she blinked; the apprentice who paid his sister's name without meaning to and could never say it again. Mothers tell these stories the way mothers everywhere tell stories about strangers' wagons. The point is *be visible, be small, be one of us.*

**The village pact** is unspoken and absolute: *we do not talk about it in the square.* If you know that the midwife is a hedge-cunning, you know it at her door, not at market. If you suspect a child is bloodline-touched, you tell their mother quietly, in the byre, not in the chapel. If a wizard rides through, you do not stare. You do not warn him off. You do not invite him in for the meal that he can afford to pay for. You **let him pass.**

**Mordren** is the template. He has rooms at the Bent Plow. Bessa serves him. Garret fixes his cart-wheel for the going rate. Bran exchanges five words with him a week. Nobody is hostile. Nobody is warm. He has not been invited to Barleynight in the two years he has been here. He has not been *uninvited* either. He simply has not been mentioned, and the absence of mention is a posture the whole village holds together without ever discussing.

## 8 · Bloodlines and inheritance

Magical talent **sometimes** runs in families. Sorcerers most reliably; Tieflings and similar by lineage definition; for other classes (wizard, cleric, druid, bard) talent is mostly trained, not inherited. The crown maintains an informal **bloodlines register** at the Office's Highmoor archive — names of families across the realm whose children have manifested across multiple generations. The register is not public. Inclusion can cost a village its midwife and a family its eldest daughter to "evaluation."

A village suspected of carrying old blood has practical reasons to keep its head down. **Lower** the visibility of the gifted child; **raise** the literacy of the gifted child anyway (the writ requires it); **never** let two of them be seen together by a sensitive.

### Known and rumored bloodlines

- **The Voss line** (rumored, Fallowton). The Voss family has farmed the north field for at least four generations. Old Henric Voss is the current head — the man who discovered the sinkhole. His grandfather **Wendel Voss** is the W in the W.V. broken dagger that may surface in the ruin ([`../../docs/dm-handbook/module-1-checks.md`](../../docs/dm-handbook/module-1-checks.md)). Wendel went down into the House of First Light sixty-to-a-hundred years ago and came back changed; the family has never explained how, and Henric does not remember being told. The bloodline is **quiet** — no Voss in living memory has cast openly — but the village whisper is that *something runs in them* and it runs toward the ruin. Henric himself fidgets a little near pale stone and does not know why.
- **The Crebb line** (known, Hollowmarch). Halsten Crebb, the warrant officer ([[crown-and-office]]), failed his son out of the licensing exam. The son ran north. The Crebbs have produced two licensed wizards and one outlaw in three generations. Halsten's tired-thigh-tap on the warrant case is a man who knows what his own family has paid.
- **The Tann line** (known, exam-pulled). Ysmae Tann is the only Tann in the registers. Her mother died of smuggling her in. The line ends with Ysmae unless she has a daughter.
- **Three or four rumored noble lines** the players will not see in Module 1 — the **Verant** royal house itself is whispered to carry old blood, which is one explanation for why the crown licenses magic instead of banning it.

## 9 · Daily magical phenomena that aren't casting

The world is **shot through** with leftover ambient magic — echoes from the Dawnweave that the broken channel did not fully seal. Most cantrips ride these echoes; that's why cantrips usually cost no strain ([[../../docs/campaign/weave-strain-magic.md]]). The leftover phenomena come in two flavors.

### Cantrips in social space

In cities, cantrips are **largely unremarked**. A market-stall mage will produce a Light cantrip to show off polished brass; a barkeep in [[geography|Marrowford]] uses Prestidigitation to chill ale on a hot day; nobody crosses themselves. In **Fallowton**, the same cantrip in the same hands would empty the room. Licensed wizards know this. **The polite cantrip** is one most villagers can pretend they didn't see: a small flame in a fingertip cupped *toward* the caster, not the audience; a Mage Hand that lifts the offered cup rather than reaching for it. The art is in cantrip-as-private-gesture. The mark of an experienced licensed wizard is that you have to be paying attention to know they cast.

### Ambient magical phenomena (locked-in examples)

Three the campaign keeps consistent. Mention them in passing; never explain.

- **The Stoneford warm-current.** Half a day west in Mereswood ([[geography]]), where the Slowmere crosses the pale stone. The water on the downstream side of the slab runs **two or three degrees warmer than it should** — never enough to thaw a winter, always enough that the swineherds' pigs choose to drink there. Nobody has ever measured. The Stoneford is a faint bleed-zone (§5).
- **The dry stone in the south hedgerow.** A single hand-sized fieldstone in the lower south hedge that **never wets in rain.** Children have known about it for generations and tested it again every spring. It came up out of the ground sometime before living memory and sits where they left it. A bird has nested on it twice.
- **The mushroom ring in the lower north field.** The fairy ring that grows every year on a piece of fallow ground the village has decided not to plant. The mushrooms are the wrong species for the soil. They come up in the same circle, six paces wide, every autumn since at least Henric's grandfather's time. The ring is roughly two hundred yards from where the sinkhole opened. Nobody has connected those two facts out loud.

These are how the world signals it **remembers being Dawnweave**. They are not casting. They are residue. Treat them like weather.

## 10 · DM use — eight moments

Where this texture surfaces at the table. Drop them in like seasoning, not lecture.

1. **The quiet after Sister Elra heals.** A PC takes a Cure Wounds from Elra inside the chapel. The two villagers in the back of the chapel **stand up and leave** without a word the moment the casting begins, and come back five minutes later. Nobody apologizes. Nobody comments. Elra does not look at the door.

2. **The seller's confession at the price.** A traveler in the Bent Plow offers a small silvered hand-mirror for two silver. When the PC asks what it does, the traveler says: *"I don't know what it does. The man who sold it to me said his wife's father had it from his own father, and the family lost the daughter who could use it three winters back. The cost is two silver. I haven't named what I think the cost was to make it."* He will not look the PC in the eye while he says it.

3. **The child showing the marks.** A baby in the village — Bessa's niece's youngest — is born with eyes a little too pale at the iris's rim. The midwife notices. The grandmother notices. The mother is told privately. The Voss women come around with a gift of cloth and do not stay long. *Nobody* speaks of it in the square. If a PC with the right background (Failed Apprentice, Ruin Scholar, Magic-Touched) goes to see the child, the mother will let them in alone and will ask, very quietly, *"Is there a way to keep this from showing?"*

4. **The polite cantrip.** Mordren needs to light his pipe at the inn. He does not produce a flame in his fingertip; he produces a flame in his **cupped palm, facing himself**, and brings the pipe to it. The flame is gone before he exhales. Bessa noticed. Bessa pretends she didn't. A PC who watched Mordren do this learned what a wizard's manners look like.

5. **The bag by the door.** A house with a known caster — including the PCs', if they're staying anywhere — has a small canvas bag packed by the back door at all times, contents: a clean shirt, a comb, a strip of jerky, six copper, and a folded paper with a name on it (the cousin in the next village who will hide the caster for one night). The bag is not discussed. Players who notice it know what it is for.

6. **The fee at Calder Vault.** A PC walks into the Sealed Court to commission an Identify. The clerk reads the posted fee — *eight silver, plus one silver per rarity band* — without looking up. When the PC produces the item, the clerk looks up *once*, looks back down, writes the rarity band on the receipt with no comment, and adds three silver. The PC has just learned that the item is **rare-tier**. The clerk has just decided not to mention it to the captain. They are even, sort of.

7. **The hedge-witch's price.** A PC seeks out a granny in Bracken Downs for a healing the licensed system would charge two silver for. The granny will take **a hen** and a promise that the PC owes her a favor "to be named when I name it." The favor is real. The granny is keeping a ledger. PCs who don't pay favors back find that the next time they need her, she's not home.

8. **The mushroom ring noticed.** A PC walks the north field with Henric Voss and Henric stops in front of the ring. He does not point at it. He says, *"Da' always told me not to plant here. Never said why."* Long pause. *"Reckon Da' didn't know neither."* Then he walks on. A PC with [[languages|Dawnweave Architect Script]] or [[../../docs/campaign/weave-strain-magic.md|Ruin Scholar]] training who looks at the ring closely sees the **six-paced circle** maps to a Converter listening-chamber pattern. They are standing two hundred yards from the House of First Light.
