# The Dawnweave Legend

The era before the seal. The civilization that built the machine under Fallowton's north field. What survived; what was paid for; what the gods cut out of the world to save what remained.

> See also: [`../../docs/campaign/weave-strain-magic.md`](../../docs/campaign/weave-strain-magic.md) (Sealing mechanics, ongoing strain), [`pantheon.md`](pantheon.md) (the four [MYTH] figures expanded here), [`calendar.md`](calendar.md) (month etymologies decoded), [`geography.md`](geography.md) (the Wealdmark, the Stoneford, the Drowned Mill, the Black Adit), [`languages.md`](languages.md) (Architect Script mechanics), [`crown-and-office.md`](crown-and-office.md) (the modern crown's blind inheritance).

## What the Dawnweave was

Imagine a world where you opened a window in winter and the lamp on the sill burned warmer for it. Where a wound bound in linen knit itself by morning because the linen was woven near a House of First Light. Where the wheat came up two weeks early on the south-facing slope below a stepped pale-stone pyramid, and the priests of that pyramid wore green-flame crowns on the feast days, and the children watched from the lower terraces and thought the priests were gods.

Imagine that world running for nine hundred years.

That was the Dawnweave at its height. Not a kingdom — a network of stepped cities arranged around the **Houses of First Light**, governed by **architect-houses** who claimed descent from the first listeners, served by a priesthood that had names for forty-three subtypes of memory. They did not call it magic. They called it **the Weft.** It moved through the world the way blood moves through a body, and they built the Converters the way a surgeon builds a tourniquet — to make the flow do what they wanted, where they wanted, for whom they wanted. The flow did. The cost was someone else's, and the someone else lived in the lower terraces.

## Timeline (against the Sealed Age, current Year 247)

The architects who survived the Sealing scattered. Most died in the Long Silence. A handful of bloodlines persist — usually marked by minor unusual traits (see §9).

## What the Dawnweave was like to live in

### Architecture

Stepped pyramids of **pale limestone** — the same stone Fallowton's sinkhole opened on, the same slab that crosses the Slowmere at the Stoneford, the same that the Drowned Mill in Cinderfen was set into. The Wealdmark was where the quarries were. The stone is not extraordinary by itself; what mattered was the **cutting.** Every block was scored along the Architect Script's concentric-ring patterns so that when assembled, the Converter's shell *was* the instruction — the building was the spell.

Houses of First Light were stepped on the outside, hollow on the inside, with five descending chambers — the topmost open to the sky, the deepest sealed below the local water table. The outer terraces caught and channeled daylight downward; **Greenstir** (month 3) is "Greening-stair" because the staircases were said to flower at first light, which they did, because their stones were eating the memories of people in the lowest chamber.

Lesser architecture in Dawnweave cities echoed the same logic on smaller scales: light-channeling courtyards, lamp-funerary halls, listening-rooms in private homes where the wealthy could hear the ambient Weft running past their walls like a brook.

### Governance

The Dawnweave was not a kingdom. It was a confederation of **architect-houses** — extended bloodlines that claimed descent from one or another of the early listeners. The four houses whose names survive in modern myth are listed in §5; eight or nine others existed at the height and are no longer remembered.

Each architect-house governed one or more Converter cities. A house was not "noble" in the modern sense. They were **priest-engineers**: practitioners who could design, commission, and operate a Converter, and who passed the technical knowledge father-to-daughter-to-grandchild for two dozen generations. The houses convened a **Council of Listeners** every seventh year at a city now lost — probably under what is now Highmoor, ironically — to coordinate the network. Disputes between houses were settled by audit of the Weft flow; the loser paid by re-anointing the winner's Converter.

There was no single sovereign. There was, by the Closing, something close: the **Hand of the Council**, an elected first-among-equals architect who could speak for the whole confederation. The Hand at the time of the Closing was a Soun-Moon descendant whose name does not survive. The Sealing erased it deliberately.

### Daily life, lower terraces

Most people in a Converter city lived in the **lower terraces** — the residential rings below the pyramid. They were not slaves in the legal sense. They were citizens. They had families, festivals, trades. They tithed memory to the local House of First Light the way modern villagers tithe barley: a portion of each year's "yield," recorded in the priest-architects' ledgers, sometimes paid in dreams, sometimes in the name of a dead relative, sometimes — in bad years — in a person.

A lower-terraces family in a bad year lost a grandmother. Not to death. To **unremembering.** Her chair stayed empty. The space at the table stayed set. They could not say what they had lost, only that something had gone. The next year the wheat came up early on the south slope.

This was the system. It was justified. Everyone benefited, the priests said. The cost was distributed.

### Daily life, upper terraces

The privileged — architect-house descendants, senior priesthood, allied merchants — lived in the **upper terraces**, ate from un-blighted fields, watched the green-flame ceremonies from the inner gallery, and lived twice as long as the lower terraces did. They believed they were the chosen of the Weft. They were, in fact, the only ones not paying for it.

They were not cruel. Most of them were sincerely religious. They prayed. They wept at funerals. They believed the lower terraces' losses were the Weft's choice, not theirs. The architecture made it easy to believe.

## The Converter system

### What a Converter physically was

A **buried five-chambered pyramid** of pale stone, scored with Architect Script, surrounded by lower-terraces residential rings and upper-terraces galleries. From above: a stepped temple. From below: a machine. From the listening-chambers in between: both, indistinguishable.

The five chambers, top to bottom:

1. **The Daylight Sanctum** — open to the sky. Where the green-flame ceremonies were performed. The public face.
2. **The Lamp Corridor** — a western-facing hall where funerary and commissioning lamps were lit. (Lampset Vigil's candles are the unconscious echo of this.)
3. **The Listening Chambers** — Soun-Moon's invention. Priests sat with victims and *recorded* what was being taken: the name, the face, the smell of a mother's hair. The records were the **payment ledger.**
4. **The Focus Floor** — Reaph's blue-glowing stones, arrayed in a concentric-ring pattern matching the Script on the walls. The active engine.
5. **The Anchor Vault** — sealed below the local water table. The point of contact with the world-magic core. Only the Hand of the Council was permitted entry, and only at the seven-year audits.

### How it operated, in conceptual terms

The Converter **channeled the Weft** through its concentric rings. To do this, the priests in the Listening Chambers extracted **living memory** from victims in the lower terraces — not life force, not soul in any clean sense, but the specific, irreplaceable memory of *being someone in particular.* The victim might survive, hollowed; or die; or simply become **unremembered** by everyone who had known them. The extracted memory became the **fuel** that bent the Weft into wonders for the region around the Converter. Healed wounds. Better grain. Fewer fevers. The cost was invisible because the people paying it had been erased from the records of having existed.

A Converter operated continuously while anointed. It could be **drained** (Hollowmonth — "Hallowing-month," when the priests drew down the active charge for maintenance), **wethered** (Wether — when a chamber was un-sealed for repair), and **re-anointed** (Goldfall — the commissioning rite, the "Golden Fall"). The calendar is the operating manual; no modern villager knows.

### The known network

The Dawnweave network is reconstructed from architect ledgers — partial, contested. Probable count: **between twenty and twenty-five major Converters**, scattered across what is now the Sealed Realm and the lands beyond its modern borders. Of these, **four are confirmed in or near the Fallow Reach:**

1. **The House of First Light** — under Fallowton's north field. Buried at the Sealing. Wards cracking now. The campaign opens because of this one.
2. **The Drowned Mill, Cinderfen** — a smaller Converter that was being decommissioned at the Sealing and was incompletely sealed. Sunk into the fen. Pale stone in its foundations. ([`geography.md`](geography.md) §Cinderfen.)
3. **The Black Adit, Banthorp** (Hollowing Hills) — a deep Converter, suspected, never excavated. The miners stopped digging in 232 SA. The air smells of iron and old smoke. The party may find this in Module 2.
4. **A Wealdmark Converter** — name lost; site lost; presumed under one of the ruined keeps in the deeper Wealdmark past Greycairn. The Wealdmark was the **architectural heartland** — the quarries supplied every Converter in the network, and the largest house-seats stood here. The crown's pre-Sealing seat was an architect-city built atop or beside a Converter that died in the Sealing fires.

The **Stoneford** slab in Mereswood is not a Converter. It is a foundation off-cut — a discarded block too large to move, left where the river found it. The fact that it still channels light at solstices is incidental and bad luck for any traveller standing on it at the wrong time of year.

### The priesthood

Three orders, all extinct as living institutions; the lineages persist underground.

- **First Listeners** — Soun-Moon's order. Sat in the Listening Chambers. Drew the memory. The most prestigious and the most damaged: a Listener at the end of a long career had heard so many extracted lives that they themselves were partially unmade.
- **Lamp-Tenders** — Bright-Leaf's order. Performed the public-facing ceremonies, lit the western corridor lamps, wore the green-flame crowns. The face of the Converter.
- **Stone-Bound** — Reaph's order. Engineers. Maintained the focus stones, the Script-scoring, the structural integrity of the five chambers. The least visible and the most necessary.

### The unmade

This is the part the legend does not glamorize and the doc should not either.

The unmade were the people the Converters fed on. Most were lower-terraces citizens whose families had tithed them in bad years. Some were criminals re-routed through the Listening Chambers in lieu of execution. Some were children — the architect-houses called these the **soft tithes** because young memory was less "set" and channeled more cleanly. Some, by the Closing, were captives from outside the network — taken in raids on the world's edges, because the network's own population had begun to refuse.

They died, or they survived as hollowed shells, or they walked back out into their own villages and watched their families fail to recognize them. The Architect Script's margins are full of tally marks in their names. A Ruin Scholar reading a Converter wall is reading a roll of the dead and the dead-while-walking, written down by the people who killed them, in a language designed for the killing.

There is no record of any architect-house objecting to the practice in the High Mercy. Three Listeners are recorded as having stopped, late, and either died or vanished. Their names are scratched out in the Script — the architects' own tally of their own apostates.

## The four architects, expanded

### Soun-Moon, the First Listener

- **In the verses ([`pantheon.md`](pantheon.md)):** the architect who taught the first priests to hear the world's magic. Patron-figure of Sownmonth (month 4).
- **DM truth.** Soun-Moon was the founding architect of the First Listeners and the inventor of the **Listening Chamber.** Her doctrine — *"the Weft cannot be taken without being heard"* — was the technical and theological justification for memory-extraction. She did not believe she was committing murder. She believed she was honoring the dead by *recording* what was taken, by giving the unmade a final witness. The ledger she designed is still readable in any Converter that survives.
- **Modern memory.** Sister Elra prays in a phrasing that is half Soun-Moon's invocation. The village does not know.
- **Concrete detail (Religion / Arcana DC 17).** Soun-Moon was the **Hand of the Council** at the time of the Closing. The Hand whose name the Sealing erased. The reason her name survives at all is that her doctrine — the listening — was older than the Closing, and was preserved in the priesthood's training liturgy before the Sealing struck. The architects who came after wrote her into the public ceremonies precisely because they could not afford for her *technical* contribution to be lost. A high-roll PC may discover that **the chant Sister Elra has been speaking for forty years was originally written by Soun-Moon's own hand**, as a Listener's apology to the unmade. Elra has been apologizing to people she does not know existed.

### Bright-Leaf, the Anointed

- **In the verses.** The priest who walked into the first light and was crowned with green flame. Patron-figure of Brightleaf (month 5).
- **DM truth.** **Bright-Leaf was a title, not a name.** Every newly-commissioned Converter-priest who survived the anointing rite was crowned Bright-Leaf for that year. The first Bright-Leaf — the one the title is named after — was a Lamp-Tender priest named **Aren** whose original name is lost. He survived the commissioning of the very first House of First Light. The green flame was the visible side-effect of memory being drawn through the freshly-anointed altar; it killed every prior candidate.
- **Aren died ten years later** of a hollowing nobody could name. He had presided over four commissionings in those ten years. The hollowing was the accumulated drain of standing in the light for the rite.
- **Concrete detail (DC 17).** The Lampset Vigil is, specifically, the anniversary of Aren's commissioning — not his death, not the Sealing. A high-roll PC may piece together that the village has been quietly celebrating the moment the first Converter was *turned on* for at least eight generations, with nobody alive who remembers it. Sister Elra's "do not wake" is asking the Lamp-Tender's ghost to stay sleeping in his altar.

### Reaph, the Hand That Builds

- **In the verses.** The smith-saint who hammered the lamps of First Light. Patron-figure of Reaph (month 7).
- **DM truth.** Reaph was the **chief engineer** of the Stone-Bound order, and personally designed the focus-stone arrays in at least three Houses of First Light, including the House of First Light under Fallowton. He was Soun-Moon's cohort-mate — they trained together at the architect-academy in what is now the Wealdmark.
- **Reaph believed.** Of all the named architects, Reaph was the most fully a true believer in the Converter system. He never wrote an apologetic passage. His ledgers are pragmatic — load-bearing calculations, focus-stone resonance frequencies, recommended priesthood rotations. He thought of the unmade as a fuel cost, listed in tally columns like ink and stone.
- **Concrete detail (DC 17).** Reaph's **focus-stone reinforcement pattern** is what Tamsin the smith's hands have been unconsciously following on the spearhead in her forge for the last three months. A PC who has studied any Converter Script can recognize the pattern on Tamsin's spearhead at History DC 14. **Reaph's signature glyph is still scored into the Stoneford slab** — a high-roll PC who knows to look (Religion DC 17 + actually visiting the Stoneford) can find it, half-worn, on the upstream face. The glyph means "anchor, do not move." It was never meant to be a ford. It was meant to hold something in place. The river found it later.

### The Hollow Star

- **In the verses.** Never named in sanctioned text. Called the Nameless Hunger, the Eye-That-Closes-Last.
- **DM truth.** The Hollow Star is not Dawnweave. It is what the Dawnweave's hubris let in. It existed before the Closing; it was outside the world; it had no interest in the world until the architects' attempt to reach the core *opened a crack the Hollow Star could see through.* It fed on magic, memory, and the meaning of things. It did not arrive — it *noticed.* The distinction matters: nothing the architects did made the Hollow Star. They only made themselves visible to it.
- **Modern memory.** Folk-tales. Children's nonsense-songs in Hearthtongue whose syllables aren't quite nonsense. Mordren's bad-dream noises. The single-glyph scratches in the deepest Converter chambers — scratched *into* the Architect Script *from the inside*, by something that was trying to learn the language the architects used.
- **Concrete detail (DC 17, **only if a PC specifically asks**).** The Hollow Star has not been sealed *out.* It has been sealed *from access to the world-magic core.* The seal works against magic, against memory, against meaning. It does not work against attention. The Hollow Star is still looking. The cracking wards under Fallowton's north field are not letting it in — yet. They are *getting its attention.*

## The Closing and the Sealing

### What the architects were trying to do

By the late High Mercy, the network was failing. The lower terraces were refusing to tithe. Raids for soft tithes were becoming unsustainable. The Listeners were burning out at the prestige roles, and the priesthood was running thin. The architect-houses, under the Hand of the Council (Soun-Moon's descendant), made a decision: **reach the world-magic core directly, without the Converters as intermediary.** Without the priesthood. Without the unmade.

Their stated reason was mercy. The Closing Works were framed publicly as **the end of memory-extraction** — a final achievement that would let the Weft flow into the world freely again, without anyone paying. Some architects sincerely believed this. Some did not. The historical record is mixed and partial because the people who would have clarified were sealed in.

Their unstated reason was pride. The houses had run the network for nine hundred years. The thought of dismantling it without proving they could replace it was, for the architect-class, unbearable. To stop the Converters and admit they had been a mistake would be to disinherit themselves.

The Closing Works ran for roughly four decades. They built **direct-anchor towers** in three Wealdmark cities, designed to bypass the Converter intermediary and reach the **Anchor Vault** of the world-magic core itself. The towers were architecturally beautiful and theologically catastrophic.

### The moment they reached

On a date no calendar now records — sometime in late autumn of what would become Year 0 SA — the architects at the third tower successfully **made contact** with the world-magic core. There were perhaps four heartbeats of triumph.

Then the Hollow Star noticed.

The crack the architects had opened — between the Anchor Vault and the open air, bypassing the buffering Converter shell — was small. The Hollow Star looked through it. The architects in the tower were unmade not into hollowing but into something worse: they were *understood* by something that had no interest in them as persons, only as flavor. The tower stones forgot what they were. The Wealdmark sky, that night, went briefly *featureless* — no stars, no dark, just a textureless attention pressing on the world.

The gods responded immediately. The Sealing happened in five days.

### The five days

These are the **Sealing Days** of the calendar. Each is named for what was sealed.

| Day | Crown calendar name | What was sealed |
|---|---|---|
| **1st Hush** | First Hush | The Wealdmark towers themselves — the breach point. Voice that spoke: **Vassa**, the Lamp-Bearer. The first warding lamps were lit. |
| **2nd Hush** | Second Hush | The active Converter network — every operating House of First Light, simultaneously. Voice: **Hesh**, the Measurer. The ledger of the unmade was closed; no further extraction possible. |
| **3rd Hush** | Third Hush | The Architect Script's *spoken* form. Reading is now safe; speaking is now spell-casting. Voice: a god whose name the Sealing took — one of the architects' own patrons, sealed into silence with them. |
| **4th Hush** | Fourth Hush | The direct path to the world-magic core. The Anchor Vaults were buried. The Weft was severed from its source and routed through the broken channel that magic now flows through. Voice: **Talune**, the Quiet Door. |
| **5th Hush** | Fifth Hush | The Hollow Star's line-of-sight on the world. Not the Hollow Star itself — only its view through the breach. Voice: a chorus, possibly all surviving gods together. This is the day the modern pantheon was born — the gods that survived the act of sealing. |
| **Long Hush** | leap-year sixth | The silence that finishes a seal. People keep quiet from dusk to dusk because the wards were originally listening for themselves to close. (See [`calendar.md`](calendar.md).) |

What was lost on the third day — the silenced god, the spoken Script — is the reason no one can re-open the seal. Godspeech ([`languages.md`](languages.md)) is what was sealed *with*. The language of the lock is in the silenced god's mouth.

## What survived. What didn't.

**Survived.**
- The Architect Script, readable and dangerous to speak.
- Roughly half the Houses of First Light, buried; the rest collapsed entirely.
- The active pantheon — Vassa, Orun, Imrel, Talune, Hesh, Brenn, Mother Sown-Beneath, Kell. Eight gods.
- The bloodlines of the architect-houses, scattered. Most don't know.
- The Wealdmark stone in the world's foundations — every Stoneford, every Drowned Mill, every Black Adit, every choked quarry.
- The calendar's bones, badly translated.
- One folk-priest in Fallowton with a chant she does not understand.

**Did not survive.**
- The architect-houses as institutions.
- The First Listener, Lamp-Tender, and Stone-Bound priesthoods.
- The unmade mid-conversion — they did not die. They were caught in the seal at the moment of extraction. They are *somewhere*, neither alive nor dead, their memory neither in them nor in the Converter. The wards keeping them in place are what cracked under Fallowton.
- The silenced god of the third day.
- Free magic. Every spell now is a withdrawal through the broken channel.
- Roughly a hundred and twenty million people who lived in the network's cities at the height. Most died in the Long Silence.

## DM use — six places the Dawnweave surfaces at the table

Not lectures. Atmosphere drops. NPC remarks. Environmental detail.

1. **The miller's millstone.** Garret has scratched a second yoke-and-lightning beside Brenn's on the mill door. He won't say why. The blue light in the foundation is a Reaph focus-stone, waking up. The party will not know this until much later — but a PC with Architect Script proficiency standing in the mill can passively catch the pattern.

2. **Tamsin's spearhead.** She has been hammering Reaph's reinforcement glyph into a spearhead for three months and does not know. When she burns her hand swearing *"by Kell's red thread,"* it is because the steel has just resonated with the cracking wards under the north field. A high Insight reads her fear correctly. A Religion check identifies what she has made.

3. **Sister Elra's chant at dusk.** *"Keeper of the first light, do not wake."* A PC who can hear it in Dawnweave catches the syllable that was Aren's original name — long gone from any other record. The translation is in [`languages.md`](languages.md). The *fact* the prayer is here, in this village, on her lips, is the Dawnweave still reaching forward eight generations.

4. **The Stoneford in summer.** The party crosses dry-shod on the pale slab. A bird drops dead in the air twelve feet upstream. A PC with the Architect Script can read Reaph's anchor-glyph on the upstream face if they think to look. Nobody will think to look unless something prompts them — a remembered detail, a Mordren hint, an Insight on Tamsin's apprenticed hands.

5. **The grain-store keeper's tally.** Hesh's two-finger balance gesture is, in its origin, a Dawnweave priest's gesture to **close a Listener's ledger.** It survives in the modern grain-store as a tax-keeping superstition. A Ruin Scholar PC who has studied a Listening Chamber's records can recognize the gesture; the keeper will not know what they're talking about.

6. **The Long Hush, if Module 1 stretches.** A leap-year game opening Greenstir 22, Year 247, runs into the Long Hush four sessions later. Old villagers will not speak from dusk to dusk. Young ones will. A PC who casts magic during the Hush in any settlement risks something the DM does not explain immediately — because the seal is still *listening for itself to close*, and a fresh draw-on-the-channel rings a bell the village's grandmothers can hear in their teeth.
