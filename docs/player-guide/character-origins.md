# Character Origins · Fallowton Session 1

*DM reference — share the relevant section with each player before the session.*

Four characters. Four reasons to be at the Bent Plow Inn on the morning the sinkhole news spreads across the common room.

---

## Character 1 · The Alchemist

**~22–23 · Artificer (Alchemist subclass) · northern road-town origin**

---

Your parents made things that worked. Clocks, scales, bearings ground to tolerance — objects that obeyed the laws of physical materials and had no opinion about anything else. Their workshop in the northern road-town smelled of oil and sawdust and the patience of people who had made the same thing many times until they got it right. They expected you to inherit that patience.

You were good at it, until you were twelve and found something inside a broken piece of your father's work that had no name in any of his manuals. Not a gear. Not a spring. Something the size of a thumbnail, faintly warm to the touch, that buzzed under your skin when you held it too long. Your father took it from you and put it in the locked drawer without explaining. When you asked, he said: *"Some things break when you look too hard."*

You have been looking hard ever since.

You moved from clockwork to chemistry, then to alchemy, following the thread of what your hands already knew without being taught. You've produced small successes — bonding agents, heat compresses, preparations that work faster and stranger than they should. You think of them as early drafts. What you're really building toward, you don't have a name for yet.

Your parents wrote to say the crown's academy is still accepting engineers with your kind of record. The kingdom needs skilled hands in its service — everyone has been told this since childhood. You wrote back that you'd think about it. You haven't thought about it.

**Why you're in Fallowton:**
Three weeks ago, in a village a day's ride east, you felt something. Not with your hands — with the back of your jaw, the way you'd feel a low bell through floorboards. A hum: old, patient, and large. You followed it west. It stopped in Fallowton.

**Personality seeds:**
- What's in the locked drawer? Did you ever look?
- The crown wants engineers who serve the kingdom. What would it actually mean to serve the kingdom? Would you refuse?
- You've had small strange effects from your alchemical work — things that behaved more like magic than chemistry. Did you know? Does it worry you?

**Weave Strain note:** Your alchemical infusions brush magic without entering it fully. You've been experiencing small Weave Strain costs without a name for them. The thing in your father's locked drawer may be relevant to why you ended up here.

## Character 2 · The Traveler

**19 · Bard or Rogue · unnamed river-town origin**

---

Your family's restaurant was the only one in town, which meant your mother's cooking was everyone's memory of what good food tasted like. Your older sister learned the recipes. You learned them too — but you also learned the guests: where they came from, why they were in a hurry, what they weren't saying when they ordered the cheapest thing.

You were seven when a traveler left a half-eaten bowl on the table and walked out without paying. Your mother was going to throw it away. You ate it instead — just to see, just because it smelled like nothing on the menu. You have never been able to describe the taste accurately. Cold stone. Morning before rain. Old sweetness underneath, like something preserved that shouldn't still be good. You asked after that taste for years; no one recognized it.

Your sister runs the restaurant now. She is good at it and happy and will be happy there for the rest of her life. You left her a note, took your knife and your dancing shoes, and left before sunrise. You've been moving for eight months.

You cook and you dance and you kill pigs with a precision that surprises people who mistake slender and pretty for soft. You are not soft. You are also not religious — in a world that lights candles against old things, you have never found that candles changed much. You take the world as it comes.

**Why you're in Fallowton:**
The last person who had any idea about the taste sent you west: *"That sounds like food from the old country. Before the hills forgot."* She didn't know more than that. You kept going. Fallowton is as far west as the road goes.

**Personality seeds:**
- What did the note to your family say? Did you mean all of it?
- You don't pray. When you're frightened, what do you actually do instead?
- What's the best thing you've eaten since leaving home? What does it tell you about what you're looking for?

**Weave Strain note:** You don't use magic. Fallowton's fear of it will seem excessive to you. You may be right that the fear is weaponized by people with power. You may also be wrong that the danger is exaggerated. Both things can be true at once.

## Character 3 · The Inn Maid

**~18–20 · Ranger or Rogue · Fallowton, born here**

---

You were left at the Bent Plow Inn on a rainy night before you could walk — in a basket, on the doorstep, with a wool blanket and a dried flower no one in Fallowton could name. Clem Thorn, the innkeeper, took you in. Not out of sentiment, because Clem wasn't sentimental. He took you in because it was his doorstep and you were there, and he was the kind of man who didn't look for reasons not to.

You grew up between the kitchen and the common room. You swept before you could read, served tables before you were tall enough to reach them without a crate, and learned every name in Fallowton before you understood that most of the world had never heard of Fallowton at all. Clem died of a lung sickness three winters ago. Bessa — his wife — kept the inn. She kept you in it too, the way you keep something that was already there when everything else changed. You are the inn maid now: you pour ale, you carry plates, you hear every conversation in the common room, and you remember all of it.

The forest started calling when you were small. You answered every week as a child — slipping out at dusk to track animals through the dark until you could predict where each one would sleep. You know the edge of the Fallowton forest the way most people know their own street. You've seen pale stone under the tree roots on the north side, the same color as the stairs they found in the field after the collapse. You never mentioned it to anyone.

When Clem got sick, you stopped going to the forest. You apprenticed with the village herbalist instead — something useful, something Bessa couldn't object to. You've been useful and present and patient for three years.

**Why you're in Fallowton:**
You are already here. You've been here your whole life. But the hole in the north field opened up and strangers are going down into it, and the thought that you might be the one who stays home again — who pours ale while someone else goes underground into the thing you've been circling since childhood — you are not doing that. You are not doing that.

**Personality seeds:**
- What does Bessa think of you going? Has she said anything? Have you let her?
- The dried flower from the basket: where is it now? What do you think it is?
- What animal do you know best? Why that one?

**Weave Strain note:** The herbalist taught you to recognize and treat Weave Strain symptoms — the grey look, the cold sweat, the way people flinch when asked to remember something small. You've seen it in travelers. You've never caused it yourself. That knowledge will matter underground more than anyone suspects.

## Character 4 · The Ex-Enforcer

**26–27 · Fighter or Paladin · royal capital origin**

---

You grew up in the capital knowing exactly what it costs to live there. Your father was a royal guard by day and a fighter in the underground circuits by night — a secret your whole family knew and no one discussed, the way you don't discuss rain when you're already wet. Your mother worked in the crown's archive and read magic theory the way some people read poetry: with pleasure and no intention of acting on it, or so you thought. You grew up between two people whose private lives contradicted their official ones, and you were convinced you were different.

You graduated from the royal academy with adequate marks and insufficient connections. The nobility took the appointments with careers attached. You took what was left: a post with the Crown's Office of Enforcement — the branch that handles magic violations in the outer provinces, the one that doesn't come up at official dinners.

The law was clear: magic is dangerous. The costs are real — Weave Strain falls on the caster and sometimes on everyone nearby. The crown cannot allow untrained commoners to harm their neighbors through careless spellwork. Magic is therefore restricted to approved practitioners. In practice, this means nobility and their sponsored scholars. The rest are reminded.

For four years, you did the reminding. Most of what you found was small: a charm to ease a child's fever. A whisper that kept a fire burning through a cold night. You arrested people for these things. You told yourself the risks were real — and they were. You had seen the costs, the spillover, the way untrained magic reached beyond its caster. You told yourself the law existed for good reasons.

Then your squad found a family. A farmer, his wife, two children. The youngest was sick. The mother was casting a healing spell — and the spell was working, and the cost was spilling onto the neighbors: a headache here, a bad night's sleep there, an old woman on the next street who briefly could not remember her husband's name. Your teammate called it an assault. Within the law, that was correct.

The farmer was known. Loved. He had given food to half the street in bad winters. The village turned on your squad. In the riot, your teammate killed the farmer — using the "resistance" clause as cover, the one everyone in the Office knew was a loophole. In the chaos, you hurt someone. You don't know if they survived.

You left before your squad reported back. You walked for three days before you admitted that you were not in shock — you were leaving, because you could not make yourself believe the farmer deserved it, and you could not make yourself believe the law was simply wrong, and you could not hold both of those things in your chest and keep working.

**Why you're in Fallowton:**
You've been walking west for six weeks. Fallowton is where the road ran out. You have coin for another week, no plan, and a sinkhole that is at least something to look at that isn't the inside of your own head.

**Personality seeds:**
- What is the one enforcement action you still believe was right? Is there one?
- Your mother and her magic books: did your father know? Did she know that he knew?
- You don't know the name of the person you hurt in the riot. Do you want to?

**Weave Strain note:** You've seen Weave Strain used as the crown's primary evidence that common people cannot be trusted with magic. The argument is: the costs are real, untrained use harms bystanders, restriction is safety. You've also seen that argument used before the evidence was in — as justification, not as conclusion. You are more afraid of what people do with permission than of magic itself.
